# Part 2: The 90s Never Ended - The Mechanics That Shaped Us and the Games That Trained Machines

## Intro: Picking up the thread

Part 1 ended on the lab idea. Part 2 takes it seriously.

We are going to do the same three-step walk again - Language, Systems, AI - but with the pieces that feel more personal and more modern. This is where you see how game vocabulary turned into psychology, how mechanics turned into product design instincts, and how the hardest game problems became AI research problems.

## Language

Part 1 was heavy on coordination, ranking, fairness, and balance. This section is the rest of the map: mastery, emotion, obsession, and the culture layer that makes games feel like shared memory.

### “Carry” and “support” - roles that turned into social identity (Warcraft III mods / early DotA, 2003)

If you want a single example of gaming language becoming workplace personality typing, “carry” and “support” are strong candidates. In the early Warcraft III mod era (including early DotA-style play in the early 2000s), teams began talking about roles as long-horizon commitments: one person becomes the late-game damage engine; others enable that outcome.

The reason those words escaped is obvious: they describe a pattern in any team effort - who gets to be the headline, and who makes the headline possible.

### “Camping” as a named tactic (Doom, 1993; Quake, 1996; Counter-Strike, 2000)

“Camping” is a small word for a big idea: exploiting position, information asymmetry, and patience. In arena shooters and early FPS - Doom (1993), Quake (1996), and later Counter-Strike (2000) - players argued about camping because it raised a fundamental question: is this clever use of the rules, or bad sportsmanship?

That debate still shows up in modern systems design: do you punish low-effort strategies, or accept that the system enables them?

### “Griefing” - naming antisocial play so communities can fight it (Ultima Online, 1997; Counter-Strike, 2000)

Early online worlds forced a harsh realization: not everyone is here to “win.” Some people are here to ruin your day. In Ultima Online (1997), players learned the hard way that open systems invite bad-faith behavior, and communities needed a word for it.

By the time team shooters like Counter-Strike (2000) normalized things like team-killing and sabotage, “griefing” had become shorthand for a whole category of design and moderation problems.

### “Ninja looting” and “KS” - when rules exist, so do loopholes (EverQuest, 1999; Diablo II, 2000)

MMOs didn’t just teach cooperation; they taught the pain of shared resources. In EverQuest (1999), “ninja looting” named the social crime of taking loot against the group’s expectations, and “KS” (kill steal) named the feeling that someone profited from your work.

The terms persist because the underlying problem persists: incentives misalign, and communities invent language to enforce norms.

### “Cheese” strategies - winning with something that feels wrong (StarCraft, 1998; Age of Empires II, 1999)

Every competitive system creates a space where something works even if it feels morally questionable. RTS communities gave that space a name: “cheese.” In StarCraft (1998) and Age of Empires II (1999), cheese strategies were early, unorthodox attacks that punished greed or inattention.

The term persists because it maps to systems: loopholes and tactics that are “within the rules” but outside the spirit.

### “Kiting” - damage while staying out of reach (EverQuest, 1999)

“Kiting” became common knowledge through EverQuest (1999) because it described a high-skill survival tactic: keep an enemy chasing you like a kite on a string while you whittle it down from range.

The word survived because it describes a general interaction pattern in systems: maintain distance, control pacing, win through mobility rather than raw power.

### “Rubber-banding” / catch-up AI as a universal complaint (Mario Kart 64, 1996; Need for Speed, 1994-1998)

If you’ve ever said “the game is cheating,” you’ve probably been describing rubber-banding: the system quietly helping opponents stay close so the race stays dramatic. Mario Kart 64 (1996) is the clean cultural reference point for most people, and the broader Need for Speed era made the pattern familiar in a more “sim” costume.

The phrase persists because it names a design tradeoff we see everywhere: fairness vs excitement, skill purity vs entertainment.

### “Bunny hopping” / movement tech as named mastery (Quake, 1996; Half-Life, 1998)

Quake-era movement produced a strange truth: sometimes the “fastest” way isn’t the “intended” way - and players will turn it into craft anyway. Terms like bunny hopping and movement “tech” show up because games like Quake (1996) rewarded timing, momentum, and physics quirks, and Half-Life (1998) inherited that DNA.

The language persists because it maps to a broader pattern: users will always search for skill ceilings the designers didn’t explicitly label.

### “RNG” - randomness as something you can blame (Diablo II, 2000; Pokémon Red/Blue, 1996)

Games have always used randomness, but loot-heavy and crit-heavy design made it conversational. In Diablo II (2000) - and in a softer form in Pokémon Red/Blue (1996) - players started talking about “RNG” as if it were an entity with moods.

The phrase persists because it gives people a way to name uncertainty in any repeatable system: I did the right thing; probability didn’t cooperate.

### “Loot” and “drop rate” - probability talk as everyday language (Diablo, 1996; Diablo II, 2000)

Diablo-style games trained people to think in probabilities long before they called it “behavioral economics.” In Diablo (1996) and Diablo II (2000), “loot” wasn’t just treasure; it was a system of chance, scarcity, and hope.

Players started speaking in “drop rates,” “runs,” and “RNG” because the game forced that mental model.

### “Grinding” and “farming” as a universal metaphor for repetitive progress (Diablo II, 2000; EverQuest, 1999; Pokémon Red/Blue, 1996)

If you grew up in the EverQuest (1999) and Diablo II (2000) era, “grind” wasn’t theoretical - it was a lifestyle: repeat the loop to gain XP, gold, drops, or levels. Pokémon Red/Blue (1996) gave a gentler version (leveling in tall grass), but the same idea: repetition buys power.

Today people grind interview prep, grind gym routines, grind tickets, grind side projects. The gaming meaning stuck because it’s brutally honest: sometimes progress is just repetition with a purpose.

### “Level up” and “XP” as the default metaphor for self-improvement (Pokémon Red/Blue, 1996; Final Fantasy VII, 1997)

“Level up” is now a motivational poster phrase - which is hilarious, because it started as a literal mechanic. In Pokémon Red/Blue (1996) and Final Fantasy VII (1997), XP made improvement visible: effort, numbers, new capabilities.

That model is sticky. It’s why people talk about “leveling up” in careers, relationships, and hobbies - because games gave us a clean, satisfying way to imagine growth as something you can earn.

### “Boss fight” as a metaphor for concentrated difficulty (Doom, 1993; Final Fantasy VII, 1997)

The idea of a “boss” is older than the 90s, but games like Doom (1993) and Final Fantasy VII (1997) made boss fights feel like punctuation: the system stops pretending it’s casual, and demands mastery.

That’s why the phrase survives outside games - because everyone recognizes the pattern.

### “Quick-save” and “save scumming” - iteration as a playstyle (Quake, 1996; Half-Life, 1998)

PC games quietly trained a generation on a powerful idea: iterate cheaply. With quick-save culture in games like Quake (1996) and Half-Life (1998), you could try something risky, rewind, and try again - not because you were dishonest, but because exploration is how you learn.

The language (“save scumming”) carries judgment, but the behavior is basically modern engineering: run experiments, roll back, learn fast.

### “One more turn” - the phrase that became a design accusation (Civilization II, 1996)

Long before doomscrolling had a name, strategy games taught people how to rationalize staying. Civilization II (1996) turned “one more turn” into a predictable self-lie - and the phrase survived because it names a universal design trick: make the next unit of engagement feel small, then chain it.

### “Speedrun” - turning completion into a competitive genre (Doom, 1993; Super Mario 64, 1996)

Speedrunning existed as a practice earlier, but communities around games like Doom (1993) (with time-based runs) and later Super Mario 64 (1996) helped turn it into a named genre with rules, categories, and proof.

The word persists because it describes a modern obsession: mastery through constraints, measured by time.

### “Tilt” - emotional state as a performance variable (StarCraft, 1998; Counter-Strike, 2000)

“Tilt” didn’t start in games (it’s older, with roots in poker and even pinball), but competitive gaming gave it a second life because it described a familiar loop: frustration, worse decisions, more frustration. In ladder cultures like StarCraft (1998) and early team shooters like Counter-Strike (2000), “tilt” became a practical warning label: you’re still playing, but your brain has left the room.

### “Ragequit” and the normalization of emotional exit (Street Fighter II, 1991; Quake, 1996; Counter-Strike, 2000)

“Ragequit” is more than a joke; it’s a behavior pattern that got named early because games made it visible. Competitive loops - from Street Fighter II (1991) arcade rivalries to Quake (1996) frag culture to early Counter-Strike (2000) servers - produced the same sequence: loss, tilt, abrupt exit, social consequence.

Once you have a word for it, you start seeing it everywhere: closing tabs, dropping calls, leaving threads. Emotional disengagement at digital speed.

### “Clutch” and “ace” - hero moments as a named unit of story (Counter-Strike, 2000)

Competitive games didn’t invent hero narratives - but they industrialized them. In Counter-Strike (2000), “clutch” became a way to label a specific kind of drama: you’re outnumbered, and you still win. “Ace” (one player takes everyone) compresses an entire highlight reel into one word.

Those terms stick because modern competitive culture runs on shareable micro-stories.

### “I’m here to kick ass and chew bubblegum…” (Duke Nukem 3D, 1996)

The phrase still shows up in memes and comment threads - but for a lot of people it survived because a video game made it portable. In Duke Nukem 3D (1996) the line isn’t just quoted; it’s performed as character.

Games became quote amplifiers - a pre-social-media distribution network for one-liners - turning a film quote into a durable gamer catchphrase, and then into a meme template that persists long after most of the original context faded.

### “Easter egg” - hidden intent as a feature (Doom, 1993; Duke Nukem 3D, 1996)

Easter eggs predate the 90s, but 90s PC games normalized the expectation that creators leave hidden jokes, secrets, and references in the world. Doom (1993) secret areas and Duke Nukem 3D (1996) pop-culture nods trained players to look for intent behind the surface.

That habit carries into modern software, where hidden shortcuts, debug pages, and undocumented behaviors are often treated as folklore.

### “Gib” - a gruesome verb that became a tech joke (Doom, 1993; Quake, 1996)

“Gib” (short for “giblets”) became FPS slang for exploding an enemy into chunks - a visceral artifact of Doom (1993) and Quake (1996) gore culture. It stuck around because it’s vivid, a little juvenile, and oddly useful as metaphor.

Engineers still occasionally say things “got gibbed” when a process or deployment blows up spectacularly.

## Systems

In Part 1, Systems was about distribution, extensibility, measurement, and “feel.” Part 2 keeps going where it gets more interesting: the tricks that turned constraints into craft, and the mechanics that quietly trained our product instincts.

### Rocket jumping and the lifecycle of an exploit (Quake, 1996)

Rocket jumping is the cleanest origin story for how systems evolve: a mechanic emerges accidentally from physics, players discover it, it becomes part of the skill ceiling, and eventually designers stop fighting it and start designing around it.

In software terms, it is the story of half our "features": someone used the system a weird way, and now we support it because the weird way is valuable.

### Duke's Build engine and representation tradeoffs (Duke Nukem 3D, 1996)

Build sits in a fascinating middle ground: 3D enough to feel like a world, but structured around representations that made authoring tractable.

That is a recurring engineering pattern: choose a representation that simplifies creation, then hide the constraints behind experience. Not maximum fidelity - maximum usable power.

### Prince of Persia and "feel" as a quality metric (Prince of Persia, 1989; legacy into the 90s)

Prince of Persia is technically a predecessor, but its influence bleeds into the whole era: movement that looks human, timing that feels intentional, animation that carries weight.

The lasting lesson is not rotoscoping. It is that feel became a product requirement. Modern games and modern UI animations still live under that rule: if it feels floaty, people do not trust it.

### Aladdin and the rise of production pipelines (Aladdin 2D platformer, 1993)

The 90s Aladdin platformer is a clean example of games behaving like production studios. The enduring idea is not just "nice animation." It is that tooling and pipelines became the multiplier: repeatable processes that turn drawings into frames into interactive motion.

The modern parallel is obvious: build the pipeline, not just the asset.

### Atmosphere as a product feature (Gods, 1991; Doom, 1993)

Gods sits in an era where vibe was engineering: art direction, music identity, coherence under constraints. The lasting concept is that atmosphere is not decoration. It is part of the product.

Modern software still chases the same thing: tools that feel like worlds, not just interfaces.

### Staged encounters and "AI that feels convincing" (Half-Life, 1998)

Half-Life is a hinge game. Its enemies were not deeply intelligent. The game sold the illusion through staged sequences, squad behaviors, and pacing.

That is still how many systems work today: not perfect autonomy, but carefully staged behavior that creates a believable experience.

### Half-Life and the wait-and-watch tactic (Half-Life, 1998)

Your scenario is the purest form of "player as system hacker": trigger an encounter, hide, let factions resolve, then clean up survivors. Half-Life made this feel legitimate because it staged real faction conflict as part of the world, not as a cutscene.

The deeper point is why this sticks: multi-agent systems can produce outcomes even when the player does nothing. Observation becomes a move.

### Pursuit and escalation as a feedback system (Need for Speed, late 90s; GTA, late 90s)

Need for Speed made pursuit feel like a controllable escalation: actions increase pressure, pressure escalates response. GTA's wanted level expresses the same idea as a meter you can read.

This pattern persists far outside games: rate limiting, abuse detection, progressive enforcement, and trust systems all use the same loop. Behavior raises heat. Heat changes the world.

### Readable state machines as the ancestor of modern game AI (Pac-Man, 1980; scaled through the 90s)

Pac-Man predates our cutoff, but it is too foundational to skip as an ancestor. Ghosts switching modes is a clean demonstration of state-driven behavior: readable, predictable, and still capable of surprise.

The 90s scaled that idea into bigger worlds. The important bridge is that readability is a design feature. When behavior is legible, players learn. When behavior is legible, systems become easier to test, tune, and eventually automate.

## AI

Part 2 is where the AI thread stops being a teaser and becomes the main act.

Games did not just give AI researchers a sandbox. They gave them a set of problems that look a lot like the problems we now ask agents to solve in the wild: navigate, plan, cooperate, infer, and adapt.

### Quake III navigation: pathfinding as a reusable subsystem (Quake III Arena, 1999)

Quake III forced AI engineering to be explicit about space. You cannot fake movement in 3D. You need representations of traversable areas, reachability, and tactical positioning.

That work hardened into a durable idea: navigation is a subsystem, not a pile of hacks. It became a standard component in games, simulators, robotics demos, and any agent that needs to move with intent.

### Build orders as programs: planning under constraints (StarCraft, 1998; Age of Empires II, 1999)

RTS games taught humans to think in executable plans. Build orders are basically programs: timed, resource-constrained sequences with tradeoffs and contingencies. A small change early can cascade into everything.

That structure is why RTS keeps showing up as "hard" for AI. The challenge is not just control. It is long-horizon planning with incomplete information and opponents who adapt.

### Multi-agent worlds: coordination, deception, and emergent strategy (Counter-Strike, 2000; StarCraft, 1998)

Single-agent problems are tidy. Team games add coordination, misdirection, and shared consequences. Team shooters like Counter-Strike (2000) and RTS like StarCraft (1998) turned coordination into a first-class skill: communicate, anticipate teammates, mislead opponents, and handle the fact that your plan depends on other minds.

That is why game AI started to feel relevant to modern agents. The difficulty is not only the environment. It is the presence of other agents with their own incentives.

### Scripted behavior vs learned behavior: believability as a design target (Half-Life, 1998; The Sims, 2000)

Half-Life proved you can create the feeling of intelligence with staged encounters and well-timed behavior. The Sims (2000) showed a different angle: a world where lots of simple rules can produce convincing social patterns.

AI research and product development still wrestle with the same tension. Do you want a system that is optimal, or one that feels coherent and trustworthy to humans?

### StarCraft to SC2LE to AlphaStar: when game AI became a headline (StarCraft franchise; StarCraft II as research platform)

RTS became a proving ground because it combines long horizons, huge action spaces, partial information, and multi-agent interaction. The StarCraft franchise provided the cultural context, and later research platforms built on that foundation turned it into a benchmark.

This is the point where the blog's arc tightens: the same competitive instincts that produced "meta" and "build orders" also produced a standardized arena for measuring agents.

### What survives in the LLM era: tool use, rewards, and "watching" as an action (Half-Life, 1998; Civilization II, 1996)

What carries over into modern LLM agents is a mindset: define goals, expose tools, measure outcomes, and learn from repeated loops.

And some of the most "agentic" behavior looks suspiciously like that Half-Life wait-and-watch tactic: trigger a system, observe how other agents resolve it, then intervene at the moment it matters. Sometimes the smartest move is not acting. It is choosing when to act.

## Closing: closing the loop, opening the next

Part 1 showed how games trained us to coordinate and measure. Part 2 showed how the deeper mechanics trained our instincts, and why research keeps coming back to the same worlds.

If there’s a Part 3 in this series, it is less about nostalgia and more about governance: moderation, incentives, economies, and the way online worlds taught us what goes wrong at scale.
