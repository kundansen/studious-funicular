# The Kitchen Counter Effect

*March 14, 2026*

*Published on Medium: https://medium.com/@kundansen/the-kitchen-counter-effect-0cda01a40ce6*

## Why Unconventional Workspaces Produce Better Ideas

I spent considerable money building a home office. Dual 4K monitors. Ergonomic everything. A desk positioned to catch natural light. The kind of setup that makes productivity blogs weep with envy.

And yet, some of my best ideas come at kitchen counters, in airport waiting areas, and at coffee shops where I have to perch on a stool that wasn't designed for human comfort.

This wasn't laziness. It was a pattern I kept noticing. And it took me a while to understand why.

## The Problem with Optimization

The optimized space is designed for efficiency. It removes friction. It allows you to sit down and immediately enter a state of flow. Your body knows this place. Your brain knows this place. Everything in the environment says: *this is where we do familiar work*.

And that's exactly the problem when you need to think creatively.

Sarnoff Mednick's Associative Theory of Creativity (1962) proposes that creative thinking fundamentally involves forming novel connections between disparate concepts. Your brain is essentially a pattern-matching engine that works best when forced into unfamiliar territory—when it can't autopilot.

The optimized home office is designed to prevent exactly this friction. It's designed so your brain can autopilot through the work. That's great for code review. It's terrible for ideation.

## The Role of Constraint

There's research suggesting that **moderate ambient noise—around 70 decibels—actually enhances abstract thinking**. Not silence. Not chaos. Moderate, unpredictable stimulation.

A kitchen counter provides this. You're slightly uncomfortable. The environment is mildly distracting. You have to think around the discomfort rather than sinking into automaticity. Your brain can't find a shortcut because it doesn't know this space well enough to have shortcuts.

This is the opposite of what we've optimized for. We've built environments that let us work *without* thinking, and then we're surprised when we don't think creatively in them.

## Routine vs. Creative Work

Here's the key distinction: **routine work and creative work have opposite environmental preferences.**

Routine work—code reviews, email, administrative tasks—benefits from the familiar, optimized space. You want friction-free execution. You want to autopilot.

Creative work—ideation, writing, architectural design, problem-solving—benefits from constraint. You want your brain forced into engagement. You want the unfamiliar that prevents shortcuts.

Most of us have only one office. So we've built an office for routine work, then wonder why creative breakthroughs don't happen there.

## The Remote Work Debate (Reframed)

The office versus work-from-home debate has missed the actual variable. It's not about office or home. It's about **familiar versus unfamiliar environments**.

Someone working from the same desk every day—whether at home or in an office—faces identical cognitive patterns as someone in a cubicle working in the same space for five years. The geography is different, but the automation is the same.

A person who works in a coffee shop one day, a library the next, their kitchen counter the day after, and their office only when doing deep review—that person is rotating between familiar and unfamiliar. And the rotation itself becomes the constraint that prevents cognitive autopilot.

## The Temporary Nature of Novel Spaces

Here's the catch: the effect is temporary. Once a new space becomes routine—once you've worked there enough times that your brain builds shortcuts—the creative benefit diminishes.

This is why working at the same coffee shop five days a week eventually becomes as autopilot as the home office. Your brain learns the space. The novelty and constraint fade.

The implication is counterintuitive: **you need to keep rotating**. Once a space becomes routine, move to a different one.

## What I Do Now

I maintain two environments:

**The optimized space** (home office with the dual monitors) for routine work. Reviews, email, administrative tasks, deep code reading. Work that benefits from friction-free execution.

**A rotation of unfamiliar spaces** (different coffee shops, libraries, kitchen counter, airport lounges) for creative work. Writing, ideation, architectural thinking, problem-solving. Work that benefits from constraint and unavoidable engagement.

When I notice that a "novel" space has become familiar—when I find myself sinking into autopilot there—I rotate to a different one.

It feels less efficient. It requires more friction. You can't sink into a perfect flow state because the environment won't let you.

That's exactly the point.

## The Productivity Inversion

We've spent decades optimizing for efficiency—removing friction, creating perfect spaces, streamlining workflow. For certain kinds of work, this is right. For others, it's actively counterproductive.

The best creative work doesn't come from perfect optimization. It comes from constraint. From discomfort. From being forced to engage deliberately because the autopilot isn't available.

The kitchen counter effect isn't about the kitchen counter. It's about what happens when your brain can't pretend to know what to do and has to actually think.
