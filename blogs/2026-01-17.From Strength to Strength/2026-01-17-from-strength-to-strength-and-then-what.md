# From Strength to Strength, and Then What

*January 17, 2026*

---

I picked up **From Strength to Strength** (Arthur C Brooks) completely by chance, following a random recommendation that surfaced on Libby. It was a welcome tangent from a long run of data-driven, habit-forming books I had been moving through, Malcolm Gladwell and adjacent territory, where patterns, metrics, and behavioral explanations were the focus. This book was very different, and I ended up learning a lot.

What surprised me most was never having come across the term "second curve" until now. I had never given conscious thought to the decline in control or delivery that comes with age, or the natural mental resistance that grows against it. Brooks argues that this phase arrives earlier than most people expect, often somewhere in their forties, definitely in their fifties. I am squarely in that range, which made the point trigger me a lot more.

The book framed this shift as mechanical rather than personal. Ability, effort, and reward no longer line up the way they once did. If I had understood this earlier, I would not have changed everything. I would have made a few decisions with more intention and less quiet confusion.

Once the idea of the second curve is on the table, other ideas begin to cluster around it. Brooks does not package them as a system or a model. They surface gradually, via stories, research, and his own experiences. I noticed myself slowing down while listening, letting certain concepts sit longer than usual, or rewinding to listen to a section again to better absorb it.

One minor personal regret was the audiobook experience itself. The narration was uneven and occasionally distracting. It was only marginally better than the machine-read audiobooks available on Safari Bookshelf. Enunciation and delivery matter more than we often admit, especially for reflective material. I nearly abandoned the book midway, but the content itself was compelling enough to carry me through.

---

### Success addiction and the hedonic treadmill

Brooks's phrasing of "success addiction" was a lovely new concept for me - simple and immediately easy to understand once you hear it. I had never thought of success as something you could become dependent on. Failure, burnout, or ambition, sure - made sense. Addiction used for success felt excessive, almost melodramatic, until I went through his explanation.

> "Success is a wonderful servant but a terrible master."

Success has a way of quietly rewriting its own terms. A meaningful milestone slowly becomes the new baseline. The satisfaction fades faster each time, and the pressure to repeat the performance increases. You are no longer chasing success - you are now maintaining the "success high".

Brooks uses the phrase "hedonic treadmill" to supplement his topic on success addiction. Achievement moves the goalposts forward, keeping motion alive while satisfaction stays oddly flat. You do not feel miserable, but you rarely feel settled either. It is a strangely neutral emotional state, filled with activity and light on meaning. You feel compelled to hit goals that you made up in your head because you got addicted to the pattern of hitting goals and claiming wins.

Brooks does not present this as a moral weakness. He treats it as a predictable psychological response to a long-term pattern of repeated achievement.

---

### Time as a shrinking asset

Another idea that was a more bitter pill than expected: Brooks's emphasis on time. He says it's all about (the loss of) time - more than productivity, energy, or even talent. He is almost stubborn in insisting that time should be treated as the primary constraint, before it feels scarce.

> "Allocate time well before you are out of time."

I am often guilty of treating time as something I could rebalance later. Relationships could wait. Taking time to reflect might wait. Even enjoyment could wait. The assumption was always that future flexibility would compensate for present neglect. Brooks makes it clear that this assumption rarely holds.

What changes with time is not just availability, but optionality. Early on, postponement is usually reversible; at a certain point, it becomes permanent and irreversible.

---

### The second curve and changing rewards

This leads naturally into Brooks's idea of the second curve. It's not about giving up or stepping back. Instead, it's about how value gently evolves and transforms over time.

> "The first curve rewards raw intelligence. The second curve rewards wisdom."

The problem, Brooks argues, is that most of us keep using first-curve metrics well into second-curve territory. We expect speed, novelty, and visible wins to continue delivering satisfaction. When those signals fade, we assume something has gone wrong.

What I appreciated was his refusal to dramatize this. There's no need to reinvent yourself or give up on your career or ambitions, rather adjustments to how to execute on them. A career change does not have to be a straight line. In many cases, it probably should not be.

> "A career change doesn't have to be a straight line."

That idea gave permission to bend without breaking. The second curve is not about doing less. It is about being rewarded differently for effort.

---

### When the work must be the reward

From there, another idea follows almost unavoidably. Eventually, the work itself has to carry its own weight. External validation tends to lose its impact more quickly than we often expect. Titles and praise stop compensating once engagement fades.

> "The work you do has to be the reward."

This is where many familiar slogans start to fall apart. Finding work you love sounds comforting, but it is not especially useful. Love implies permanence and emotional consistency. Work rarely behaves that way over the course of decades.

I find Brooks's alternative perspective more relatable. Just aim to do the most engaging work you can.

> "Do the most interesting thing you can."

Interesting work does not promise ease or constant enjoyment. It promises engagement. It gives you a reason to maintain curiosity even when outcomes disappoint. That detail made sense to me, especially when considering longevity over short-term motivation.

---

### Friendship as chosen infrastructure

Brooks presents research suggesting that chosen friendships correlate more strongly with well-being than relationships with adult children. I had to stop and replay that section.

> "Contact with friends is more strongly correlated with well-being than contact with adult children."

The main idea isn't about reducing the importance of family, but rather highlighting how valuable the relationships we choose freely can be. It's a reminder to cherish those connections we actively build and nurture. Brooks turns to Aristotle's ladder of friendship to explain why this matters. Utility friendships help us get things done. Pleasure friendships make life enjoyable. Perfect friendships involve willing the good of the other, for their own sake.

> "Perfect friendship is willing the good of the other for the other's sake."

It is uncomfortable to admit how rare that third category becomes in adult professional life. These friendships demand time without obvious return. They resist optimization. They look inefficient on a calendar, which makes them easy to neglect.

Brooks sees this not as sentimentality but as an important structure. When professional identities shift, relationships chosen freely often carry more emotional importance than those defined by obligation.

---

### Aristotle's ladder of friendship

One of the more unexpected detours in the book led me back to **Aristotle**, and his surprisingly practical view of friendship. Aristotle described friendship as existing on three distinct levels, each serving a different purpose.

The first level is friendship of utility, where people are connected by mutual benefit. These are deal friends, colleagues, collaborators, and people whose presence makes life or work function more smoothly.

The second level is friendship of pleasure, built around shared interests, humor, admiration, or enjoyment. These friendships feel warmer and more human, often tied to hobbies, conversations, or social chemistry. Many adult friendships live comfortably here, and there is nothing shallow about that.

The third level is what Aristotle called perfect friendship. This is not about usefulness or enjoyment, but about wishing the good of the other person for their own sake. These friendships are rare, chosen, and slow to form. They require time, vulnerability, and a willingness to show up without a role to play.

Adult life guides us into utility and pleasure friendships. Utility friendships are reinforced through professional ties, while pleasure friendships survive when schedules permit but seem optional. Perfect friendships face challenges from busy calendars focused on efficiency. Aristotle noted that as we age, friendships become less transactional, making them harder to fit into a life of constant optimization. These deep relationships are difficult to scale or justify, which is why they truly matter.

Brooks uses Aristotle to explain that as careers change and identities shift, friendships based solely on utility often diminish. What endures are relationships freely chosen, without obligation or benefit. These relationships carry a different significance. Reading this part made me rethink how I've casually classified friendships over time. Not every relationship has to advance upward. However, ignoring the highest rung altogether can incur a cost that reveals itself later. Aristotle's ladder is more about understanding than judgment.

---

### Investing time, effort, and affection

All of this culminates in Brooks's idea of intelligent investment of personal allocation. Time, effort, and affection are the currencies we actually spend every day. Most of us invest heavily in effort because effort produces visible results.

> "Invest intelligently in time, effort, and affection."

Affection, by contrast, compounds slowly and quietly. It does not announce its returns. Early success trains us to discount it, because effort seems more productive. Over time, this unevenness leaves meaning exposed and fragile.

---

### Graceful continuation

The book ends without fireworks, which felt appropriate. There is no call for immediate action, checklists, or dramatic identity shifts. Brooks neither pontificates nor judges - he explains rather matter-of-factly.

> "Happiness is not about becoming someone new. It is about becoming more yourself."

He calls it a graceful continuation. Staying useful without holding on to obsolete and/or deceptive measures of worth. Accepting that certain strengths fade in us while others emerge. Letting ambition persist without demanding constant proof.

For a book I came across almost by accident, I learnt a whole set of new phrases and concepts. Not bad at all!
