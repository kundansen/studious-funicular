I picked up From Strength to Strength almost by accident, following a random recommendation that surfaced on Libby. It was a welcome tangent from a long run of data-driven, habit-forming books. I had been moving through Malcolm Gladwell and adjacent territory, where patterns, metrics, and behavioral explanations dominate. This book landed differently, and I noticed that immediately.

What surprised me most was not learning about the second curve itself. I had not encountered the idea before, nor the expected resistance to declining control or delivery. Brooks argues that this phase arrives earlier than most people expect, often somewhere in their forties. I am squarely in that range, which made the point land closer to home than I expected.

The book reframed this shift as mechanical rather than personal. Ability, effort, and reward no longer line up the way they once did. If I had understood this earlier, I would not have changed everything. I would have made a few decisions with more intention and less quiet confusion.

Once the idea of the second curve is on the table, other ideas begin to cluster around it. Brooks does not package them as a system or a model. They surface gradually, via stories, research, and his own experiences. I noticed myself slowing down while listening, letting certain concepts sit longer than usual, or rewinding to listen to a section again to better absorb it.

One minor regret was the audiobook experience itself. The narration was uneven and occasionally distracting. It was only marginally better than the machine-read audiobooks available on Safari Bookshelf. Enunciation and delivery matter more than we often admit, especially for reflective material. I nearly abandoned the book midway, but the content itself was compelling enough to carry me through.

---

Success addiction and the hedonic treadmill

Brooks’s phrasing of “success addiction” was a lovely new concept for me - simple and immediately easy to understand once you hear it. I had never thought of success as something you could become dependent on. Failure, burnout, or ambition, sure - made sense. Addiction used for success felt excessive, almost melodramatic, until I went through his explanation.

“Success is a wonderful servant but a terrible master.”

Success has a way of quietly rewriting its own terms. A meaningful milestone slowly becomes the new baseline. The satisfaction fades faster each time, and the pressure to repeat the performance increases. You are no longer chasing success - you are now maintaining the “success high”.

Brooks uses the phrase “hedonic treadmill” to supplement his topic on success addiction. Achievement moves the goalposts forward, keeping motion alive while satisfaction stays oddly flat. You do not feel miserable, but you rarely feel settled either. It is a strangely neutral emotional state, filled with activity and light on meaning. You feel compelled to hit goals that you made up in your head because you got addicted to the pattern of hitting goals and claiming wins.

Brooks does not frame this as a moral weakness. He treats it as a predictable psychological response to a long-term pattern of repeated achievement. 

---

Time as a shrinking asset

Another idea that landed harder than expected was Brooks’s emphasis on time. Not productivity, not energy, not even talent. Time. He is almost stubborn in insisting that time should be treated as the primary constraint, long before it feels scarce.

“Allocate time well before you are out of time.”

I often treat time as something I could rebalance later. Relationships could wait. Taking time to reflect might wait. Even enjoyment could wait. The assumption was always that future flexibility would compensate for present neglect. Brooks makes it clear that this assumption rarely holds.

What changes with time is not just availability, but optionality. Early on, postponement feels reversible. Later, the same postponement quietly becomes permanent.

---

The second curve and changing rewards

This leads naturally into Brooks’s idea of the second curve, which was entirely new to me. It is not about collapse or retreat. It is about how value quietly changes shape over time.

“The first curve rewards raw intelligence. The second curve rewards wisdom.”

The problem, Brooks argues, is that most of us keep using first-curve metrics well into second-curve territory. We expect speed, novelty, and visible wins to continue delivering satisfaction. When those signals fade, we assume something has gone wrong.

What I appreciated was his refusal to dramatize this. There is no mandate to reinvent yourself or abandon your career. A career change does not have to be a straight line. In many cases, it probably should not be.

“A career change doesn’t have to be a straight line.”

That idea gave permission to bend without breaking. The second curve is not about doing less. It is about being rewarded differently for effort.

---

When the work must be the reward

From there, another idea follows almost unavoidably. Eventually, the work itself has to carry its own weight. External validation wears thin faster than we expect. Titles and praise stop compensating once engagement fades.

“The work you do has to be the reward.”

This is where many familiar slogans start to fall apart. Finding work you love sounds comforting, but it is not especially useful. Love implies permanence and emotional consistency. Work rarely behaves that way over the course of decades.

I found Brooks’s alternative framing more realistic. Do the most interesting work you can.

“Do the most interesting thing you can.”

Interesting work does not promise ease or constant enjoyment. It promises engagement. It gives you a reason to maintain curiosity even when outcomes disappoint. That distinction mattered to me, especially when thinking about longevity rather than short-term motivation.

---

Friendship as chosen infrastructure

One section I did not expect to care much about focused on friendship. Brooks presents research suggesting that chosen friendships correlate more strongly with well-being than relationships with adult children. I had to stop and reread that line.

“Contact with friends is more strongly correlated with well-being than contact with adult children.”

The point is not to diminish the importance of family. It is to point out the significance of the relationships we choose freely. Brooks turns to Aristotle’s ladder of friendship to explain why this matters. Utility friendships help us get things done. Pleasure friendships make life enjoyable. Perfect friendships involve willing the good of the other, for their own sake.

“Perfect friendship is willing the good of the other for the other’s sake.”

It is uncomfortable to admit how rare that third category becomes in adult professional life. These friendships demand time without obvious return. They resist optimization. They look inefficient on a calendar, which makes them easy to neglect.

Brooks does not treat this as sentimentality. He treats it as a structure. When professional identities shift, relationships chosen freely often carry more emotional importance than those defined by obligation.

---

Investing time, effort, and affection

All of this culminates in Brooks’s idea of intelligent investment of personal allocation. Time, effort, and affection are the currencies we actually spend every day. Most of us invest heavily in effort because effort produces visible results. “Do something”, paraphrasing from Lord of the Flies.

“Invest intelligently in time, effort, and affection.”

Affection, by contrast, compounds slowly and quietly. It does not announce its returns. Early success trains us to discount it, because effort seems more productive. Over time, this unevenness leaves meaning exposed and fragile.

What I appreciated was the lack of moralizing. Brooks does not judge. He explains matter-of-factly. Meaning, in his framing, is built through allocation rather than discovered through insight.

---

Graceful continuation

The book ends without fireworks, which felt appropriate. There is no call for immediate action (CTAs), checklists, or dramatic identity shifts. Brooks offers something much quieter.

“Happiness is not about becoming someone new. It is about becoming more yourself.”

He calls it a graceful continuation. Staying useful without holding on to obsolete and/or deceptive measures of worth. Accepting that certain strengths fade in us while others emerge. Letting ambition persist without demanding constant proof.

For a book I came across almost by accident, I learnt a whole set of new phrases and concepts. Not bad at all!
