## Intro: The decade that shipped culture in executable form

Today’s topic is completely different from anything I have written before. I am writing this because we are living in a world shaped by a specific, often overlooked history: the games of the 90s.

These games created a cultural impact that went far beyond entertainment. They established words, scenarios, and behaviors that continue to exhibit themselves in our day-to-day lives—including in the most cutting-edge advances in AI. We live with these artifacts every day, often without consciously relating them back to the pivotal moments in a game involving a few hundred pixels that resulted in where we are now.

Consider this a forensic report on that cultural infrastructure. Those games exported three durable layers that still run under modern life: how we talk, how we design systems, and, unexpectedly, how we now train machines.

In Part 1 we start with the words that helped strangers coordinate at speed. Then we step into the design patterns that made those worlds scale. We end with the first AI hinge: why games were always closer to labs than we realized.

## Language

These words did three jobs: they helped strangers coordinate, they signaled status, and they argued about fairness. They also travel well. Drop them into a Slack thread and they still describe the situation.

The slang is the surface. Under it is a portable operating system for online life.

## Language

The slang of the 90s didn't just survive; it gentrified. It started as functional shorthand for teenagers screaming over 56k modems and ended up as the operating system for modern corporate communication.

### The Protocol of Speed
The early internet was chatty, but games were breathless. You didn't have time to type "I am stepping away for a moment." You typed **"AFK"**. You didn't type "I am looking for a team." You typed **"LFG"**. And when it was over, you didn't write a thank-you note. You typed **"GG"**.

This wasn't laziness; it was high-bandwidth compression. We needed a way to signal intent, availability, and sportsmanship in milliseconds. Today, that same compression runs our Slack workspaces. "AFK" is the polite way to say you're ignoring a ping. "GG" is the universal period at the end of a project. We adopted the protocol of the deathmatch because modern work feels suspiciously like one: fast, loud, and entirely text-based.

### Workplace LARPing
If you work in tech, you are playing an MMORPG. We just changed the graphics.

We literally use medieval fantasy terms to organize our labor. We join **"guilds"** (Spotify model, anyone?). We organize **"raids"** on difficult problems. We worry about **"drawing aggro"** from leadership during a meeting. We talk about **"tanking"** a difficult client so the team can work.

And just like *EverQuest*, we are obsessed with the **"Trinity"** of roles. In 1999, you were a Tank, Healer, or DPS. In 2024, you are a PM, Designer, or Engineer. The labels changed, but the anxiety didn't: if the Tank (PM) doesn't hold aggro, the Healer (Designer) gets crushed, and the DPS (Engineers) can't do their job. We aren't agile; we're just LARPing a dungeon crawl in business casual.

### The Cruelty of Competence
Finally, games gave us the vocabulary of status. Every hierarchy needs a way to punch down, and 90s gaming perfected it.

**"Noob"** wasn't just an insult; it was a diagnostic. It meant you hadn't done the reading. **"Smurfing"**—the act of a high-status player masquerading as a rookie to brutalize lesser opponents—is now the standard playbook for anonymous Twitter accounts and cynical founders launching "stealth" projects.

And then there's **"Nerf"**. It’s the only word we have for the specific pain of a system administrator deciding you are having too much fun. When an API rate-limits you, or an algorithm update tanks your reach, you haven't been "adjusted." You've been nerfed. The term survives because the feeling is universal: the gods of the system have capriciously decided you are too powerful.

## Systems

Language is the soft export. The hard export is the systems thinking. We spent a decade voluntarily submitting to aggressive feedback loops, and then we rebuilt the entire internet to mimic them.

### The Tyranny of the Scoreboard
Quake and StarCraft taught a generation that if it isn't measured, it didn't happen. The **scoreboard** was the ultimate arbiter of truth. It was brutal, public, and unimpeachable.

We took that logic and applied it to human social connection. What is a "Follower Count" if not a persistent frag count? What is a LinkedIn streak if not a grind for XP? We gamified our social standing because we were raised in worlds where numbers were the only proof of worth. We are all permanently **"LFG"** now, checking the leaderboard to see if we are winning at life.

### A Eulogy for the LAN Party
There was a time when computing was *local*. To play multiplayer, you had to physically carry a beige tower to a friend's basement, network it yourself, and debug the IP conflicts. It was a hassle, but it was *yours*.

Today, we have traded the LAN party for the cloud. It works better, sure. But we lost the agency. We don't own the server; we rent space on someone else's computer. The spirit of the LAN—local authority, physical proximity, and total control—is the ghost haunting every decentralized protocol and self-hosted server movement. We are trying to get back to the basement.

### Shareware vs. The Subscription Hell
Wolfenstein 3D gave you the whole first episode for free. It was confident. It said, "You will want this." It was the original Product-Led Growth.

Now, everything is a subscription. You don't buy the game; you rent the license. The shareware model respected the user's intelligence: try it, then buy it. The modern model respects nothing but the monthly recurring revenue (MRR). We moved from "shareware" to "ransomware-lite"—keep paying, or lose access to your tools.

## AI

Here is the twist. While we were learning to talk efficiency and think in systems, the games were doing something else. They were training the things that would eventually replace us.

### The Accidental Gym
We didn't build *StarCraft* and *Doom* for AI. We built them to blow off steam. But it turns out that if you want to teach a machine to think, a video game is the perfect Skinner Box. It has clear rules, hard metrics, and it runs faster than real time.

For the last ten years, the most advanced AI labs on the planet haven't just been feeding chatbots Wikipedia. They've been forcing them to play *Quake III Arena*. We built the cages before we built the beasts.

### The Violence of Perception
There is a profound irony that computer vision—the technology that allows cars to drive themselves and drones to navigate battlefields—cut its teeth on *Doom*.

To play *Doom*, you have to process a 2D grid of pixels, infer a 3D geometry, identify threats, and react in milliseconds. That is the fundamental loop of robotics. We taught AI to "see" not by showing it flowers, but by showing it demons. Algorithms like **ViZDoom** turned our childhood nostalgia into training data for autonomous agents. The lineage of the peaceful robot butler traces directly back to a space marine with a shotgun.

## Closing

The 90s didn't end; they just scaled up. We talk like fraggers, we work like guildmates, and we are training our replacements on the very maps we used to memorize.
