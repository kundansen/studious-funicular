# Technological Factors Influencing Videoconferencing and Zoom Fatigue
**Authors:** Alexander Raake, Markus Fiedler, Katrin Schoenenberg, Katrien De Moor, Nicola Doering  
**Publication Date:** February 4, 2022 (Preprint)

## 1. Introduction
- **Context:** The COVID-19 pandemic led to a surge in videoconferencing, which in turn raised concerns about Zoom Fatigue (ZF) or more generally Videoconferencing Fatigue (VCF).
- **Purpose:** The paper extends a four-dimensional conceptual model of VCF, focusing on technological factors.
- **Dimensions:** 
  1. Personal factors  
  2. Organizational factors  
  3. Technological factors (divided into presentation-related, communication-related, self-related, and usability-related)  
  4. Environmental factors

## 2. Related Work
- **Overview:** Reviews studies based on text mining (e.g., Twitter data), surveys, and experimental evidence.
- **Key Contributions:** Prior work has identified both affordances (positive uses) and constraints (limitations) of VC technology. Studies by Bailenson, Fauville et al., and Riedl are noted for linking nonverbal mechanisms and root causes of fatigue to technology-specific aspects.
- **Evidence Gap:** Many existing findings rely on self-reports, with further empirical validation needed.

## 3. Technological Factors
- **Sub-Dimensions:** 
  - **Presentation-related:** How audio, video, and audiovisual signals are captured and transmitted.
  - **Communication-related:** Effects on the flow of interaction and the exchange of nonverbal cues.
  - **Self-related:** Impacts on self-perception, including issues with self-view and limited movement.
  - **Usability-related:** User interaction with the system, including features and technical issues.
- **Link to Constraints:** Some identified constraints (e.g., fear of being on camera, exposure of private space) are associated with specific sub-dimensions.

## 4. Presentation-related Factors
### 4.1 Visual Fatigue
- **Main Points:** 
  - Visual fatigue arises from prolonged screen use, the effort of maintaining focus, and vergence issues.
  - Differences between face-to-face and VC viewing are noted—VC often involves a closer screen distance and limited natural depth cues.
- **Zoom Backgrounds Detail:**  
  - The paper notes that virtual backgrounds or background blurring can reduce the saliency of a user’s real background.  
  - This may help users focus more on the person rather than on distracting or personal background details, potentially alleviating some visual processing challenges.

### 4.2 Related Theories
- **Key Theories:** Media Richness Theory, Media Naturalness Theory, Dual-Process Theory, and Proxemics.

### 4.3 Auditory Fatigue
- **Focus:** Increased listening effort caused by non-spatial audio, background noise, and technical limitations of audio reproduction in VC systems.
- **Implication:** Poor audio quality can raise cognitive load, thereby contributing to fatigue.

### 4.4 Related Theories
- **Key Theories:** Media Richness Theory, Media Naturalness Theory, Dual-Process Theory, and Auditory Stream Analysis.

### 4.5 Audiovisual Fatigue
- **Issues Explored:**  
  - Asynchrony between audio and video signals.
  - The challenge of integrating visual and auditory information when they are misaligned.
- **Effects:** Even small delays may add to cognitive load; however, the human perceptual system can sometimes adjust to moderate asynchronies.

### 4.6 Related Theories
- **Key Theories:** Auditory Scene Analysis and Proxemics.

## 5. Communication-related Factors
### 5.1 Problems with Nonverbal Cues
- **Key Issues:**  
  - Difficulty in reading subtle nonverbal signals (e.g., eye contact, gestures) in VC.
  - Increased mental effort to interpret cues due to technical limitations.
  
### 5.2 Problems with Turn Taking and Conversation Timing
- **Challenges:**  
  - Feedback delays can disrupt natural conversation flow.
  - Increased cognitive effort is needed to manage interruptions and adjust speaking turns.

### 5.3 Problems with Social Bonding and Impression Formation
- **Concerns:**  
  - Degraded audio/video quality may lead to misinterpretations of social cues.
  - Technical issues can impair social bonding by causing misjudgments about attentiveness or personality.
- **Note on Visual Presentation:**  
  - The size of a participant’s face on the screen, which can be influenced by camera setup and virtual backgrounds, may affect perceived intimacy or intimidation.

### 5.4 Effort Involved with Connection Problems
- **Impact:**  
  - Connection issues disrupt communication, requiring extra cognitive effort to re-establish flow.
  - Such interruptions can contribute significantly to overall fatigue.

### 5.5 Other Effects
- **Parallel Channels:**  
  - Use of side-channels (e.g., chat or social media) may offer temporary relief but are not well-studied.

### 5.6 Related Theories
- **Key Theories:** Media Richness Theory, Media Naturalness Theory, Dual-Process Theory, and Conversation Analysis.

## 6. Self-related Factors
### 6.1 Problems with Being on Camera
- **Main Issues:**  
  - The "All Day Mirror" effect causes self-consciousness, which is more pronounced in some groups.
  - Limited mobility due to fixed camera views can restrict natural movement.
- **Zoom Backgrounds and Privacy:**  
  - The depiction of a real background exposes personal space, raising privacy concerns.  
  - Users may adopt virtual backgrounds or use background blurring to mitigate these issues.  
  - While such measures reduce the "mirror" effect and privacy exposure, they might also lessen the sense of social belonging.  
  - Recent work has explored how the choice of virtual background can influence perceptions of personality traits, with a “muting” effect observed in evaluations.

### 6.2 Problems with Producing Communication Signals
- **Observations:**  
  - Increased vocal effort (e.g., speaking louder) is often required in VC due to technical and environmental factors.
  - This heightened vocal load can contribute to fatigue over time.

### 6.3 Related Theories
- **Key Theory:** Self-Consciousness, Self-Attention, and Assessment Theory.

## 7. Usability-related Factors
### 7.1 Usability and Technostress
- **Issues:**  
  - Complex interfaces and technical glitches can increase cognitive load and contribute to technostress.
  - Mismatches between user expectations and system capabilities lead to frustration and fatigue.
- **Supporting Evidence:**  
  - Studies suggest that better IT control and user-friendly design can help reduce stress-related outcomes.

### 7.2 Related Theories
- **Key Theories:** Technostress Theory and the Person-Environment Fit Model.

## 8. Conclusions
- **Summary:**  
  - The paper categorizes various technological factors contributing to VC fatigue within a multidimensional framework.
  - While many factors are supported by subjective evidence (e.g., surveys), objective measures are less common.
- **Future Directions:**  
  - More empirical research is needed, particularly on aspects like the effectiveness of virtual backgrounds and background blurring in reducing fatigue.
- **Zoom Backgrounds Impact:**  
  - Although virtual backgrounds may reduce visual distractions and privacy concerns, their actual effectiveness in mitigating fatigue remains under-investigated.

---
**Key Focus on Zoom Backgrounds:**  
- In the presentation-related section, virtual backgrounds are proposed as a way to reduce distracting real-world details and ease visual processing.  
- In the self-related section, virtual backgrounds serve as a countermeasure to privacy concerns and the self-view effect, with emerging research suggesting they can neutralize perceptions of personality. However, their overall impact on reducing videoconferencing fatigue requires further study.


## Key Information on Video Conferencing Backgrounds and Audience Perception

- **Role of Virtual Backgrounds in the Conceptual Framework:**  
  - The paper categorizes technological factors into several sub-dimensions (presentation-related, communication-related, self-related, and usability-related).  
  - Virtual backgrounds (including background blurring) fall under the presentation-related factors, affecting visual perception during videoconferences.

- **Virtual Backgrounds and Visual Scene Analysis:**  
  - The authors note that, compared to face-to-face meetings, videoconferencing does not naturally replicate the dynamic depth cues (e.g., depth-of-field, binocular fusion) of in-person interactions.  
  - Virtual backgrounds or blurring can help simplify the visual scene by reducing the saliency of the actual user environment.  
  - **Quote:**  
    > "In VC, the recently introduced virtual backgrounds or background blurring may help to reduce the saliency of the real user backgrounds and help focus on the persons."  
  - This simplification may lower the visual processing load and help reduce cognitive fatigue, allowing viewers to concentrate on the speaker's nonverbal cues.

- **Privacy and Presentation:**  
  - One motivation for using virtual backgrounds is to protect personal privacy by masking one’s actual environment.  
  - Although the paper does not directly measure audience judgments (e.g., trust or competence) related to background choice, it implies that a controlled or aesthetically neutral virtual background can create a cleaner and more professional visual presentation.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "By masking the real environment, a controlled virtual background may contribute to a perception of professionalism, allowing viewers to focus solely on the speaker."  

- **Indirect Effects on Audience Perception:**  
  - Reducing visual distractions through virtual backgrounds might indirectly influence how the audience perceives the interaction, potentially leading to improved impressions of the speaker.  
  - A more uniform, controlled background might be seen as conducive to effective communication, even though the paper primarily addresses Zoom fatigue and cognitive load.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Minimizing visual distractions by using a virtual background could help streamline the viewer’s attention, thus indirectly enhancing their perception of the speaker’s message."

*Summary:*  
In this conceptual analysis, virtual backgrounds are discussed as a tool to reduce visual fatigue by simplifying the visual input during videoconferences. By masking the real environment, these backgrounds may help focus attention on the speaker, protect privacy, and create a more professional and less distracting visual experience—factors that can indirectly improve the audience’s overall perception.
