# Zoom Affordances and Identity: A Case Study
**Authors:** Angela M. Cirucci  
**Publication Date:** January–March 2023

## Abstract
- **Purpose:** Investigate how virtual learning environments (VLEs)—specifically Zoom—shape student identity performance.
- **Method:** Combines a walkthrough analysis of Zoom’s interface with an open-ended survey of 250 undergraduates.
- **Key Findings:** Although students find Zoom “user-friendly” (its design mimics popular social media), many feel uncomfortable showing their live faces and physical backgrounds. This discomfort is especially pronounced among students from lower-income backgrounds and BIPOC women who fear judgment based on appearance.

## 1. Introduction
- **Context:** The shift to virtual learning during the pandemic forced educators and students into digital spaces, merging home and classroom environments.
- **Research Gap:** While many studies have examined engagement in online settings, few have explored how VLE design influences student identity and self-presentation.
- **Study Focus:** The case study examines Zoom’s affordances—its design, features, and overall user experience—and how these influence identity performance and learning quality.

## 2. Virtual Learning Environments (VLEs)
- **Historical Perspective:** Early social media (e.g., MySpace) allowed personalized “bedroom” spaces where users could experiment with identity.
- **Contrast with Traditional VLEs:** Learning management systems (e.g., Blackboard, Canvas) were not designed for social identity performance.
- **Pandemic Impact:** With the move to synchronous video classes via Zoom, the boundary between private (home) and public (classroom) spaces became blurred, raising concerns about identity and privacy.

## 3. Identity Online and Affordances
- **Identity as Performance:** Drawing on Goffman, the paper argues that identity is continually managed and performed. Digital platforms influence this process by offering (or limiting) opportunities for self-expression.
- **Affordance Lens:** Affordances represent the relationship between user perceptions and design choices. Zoom’s interface encourages certain behaviors (e.g., turning on the camera) while discouraging deeper identity customization.
- **Affective Affordances:** Beyond functional features, the emotional and social cues embedded in design shape how users feel about sharing their identities online.

## 4. Method
- **Walkthrough Analysis:** The researcher conducted a systematic review of Zoom’s end-user interface (EUI) by examining layout, button/menu placement, and symbolic design cues.
- **Survey Design:** An IRB-approved, open-ended survey was administered to undergraduate students who had taken at least one virtual class during the 2020–2021 academic year. Topics included VLE preferences, camera habits, background choices, and identity personalization.
- **Demographic Focus:** The survey probed how different identities (e.g., income level, race, gender) affect perceptions and use of identity affordances in Zoom.

## 5. Findings
### User Friendly
- **Interface Appeal:** Students favor Zoom for its “user-friendly” design—its main interface uses large, simple buttons reminiscent of popular social media apps.
- **Limited Customization:** Despite this familiarity, few students engage with identity personalization options (e.g., profile pictures, “touch up” features, virtual backgrounds).

### Location Appearance
- **Privacy Concerns:** Many students feel self-conscious about classmates and professors seeing their physical backgrounds. Those from lower-income households express particular anxiety about their home environments.
- **Virtual Backgrounds:** Although available, virtual backgrounds are often hidden in the interface and sometimes signal that users are embarrassed about their actual surroundings.

### Physical Appearance
- **Appearance Anxiety:** A significant number of respondents, especially BIPOC women, report discomfort with turning on their cameras due to worries about being judged on their looks.
- **Identity Performance Gap:** Unlike social media platforms that actively encourage users to craft and display their identities, Zoom’s design discourages such personal expression, leaving many students feeling exposed and vulnerable.

## 6. Discussion
- **Mismatch of Affordances:** Zoom’s design borrows elements from social media (e.g., a simple, app-like interface) but falls short in enabling identity creation and management.
- **Impact on Marginalized Groups:** The lack of identity affordances is felt most acutely by students who are already marginalized. Their discomfort with revealing personal space and physical appearance can negatively affect their engagement and learning.
- **Role of Social Presence:** The paper argues that effective learning is tied to social presence. When students feel unable to control their self-presentation, it can diminish their overall classroom experience.

## 7. Limitations and Future Recommendations
- **Scope:** As a case study at one university, the findings may not be generalizable across all institutions or regions.
- **Further Research:** Future studies should include a broader range of institutions and compare experiences across pre-COVID, COVID, and post-COVID periods.
- **Design Implications:** Researchers recommend that universities and educators work to align technology choices with students’ identity needs. Suggestions include offering training on identity personalization and rethinking interface designs to better support self-expression.

## 8. Conclusion
- **Summary:** Although Zoom is preferred for its ease-of-use, its design fails to support robust identity performance. This leads to discomfort, particularly among students from marginalized backgrounds.
- **Call to Action:** Educators and platform designers should consider integrating identity management tools and creating a more inclusive digital learning environment that respects both course requirements and students’ personal identity needs.



## Key Information on Virtual Backgrounds and Their Impact on Identity and Perception

### Overview
- The study investigates how virtual learning environments (VLEs)—with a focus on Zoom—affect student identity performance and learning quality.
- It combines a walkthrough analysis of Zoom’s interface with an open-ended survey of undergraduate students to understand how Zoom’s affordances (including virtual backgrounds) shape student perceptions.
- Although Zoom is praised for its "user-friendly" design reminiscent of popular social media apps, it paradoxically makes users uncomfortable with being visible.

### Impact of Zoom Backgrounds on Perception and Identity
- **Discomfort with Exposing Physical Backgrounds:**
  - Many students are uneasy about their classmates and professors seeing their personal, physical environments.
  - This discomfort is particularly pronounced among students with lower family incomes and among BIPOC women.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Students were concerned about their classmates and professors seeing their physical backgrounds, particularly those who reported having family incomes below $50,000."
  
- **Lack of Identity Affordances:**
  - Zoom’s design does not actively encourage users to personalize their appearance or environment. Unlike social media platforms where identity performance is integral, Zoom’s interface discourages turning on cameras and sharing one’s live background.
  - This absence of personalization limits students’ ability to express their identities during online classes.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Zoom seems to discourage identity performance norms that are common via popular social media apps, making users perceive the act of turning on cameras and revealing their live backgrounds as uncomfortable and disconnected from their academic needs."

- **Gendered Perceptions:**
  - The study found that BIPOC women, in particular, feel vulnerable to judgment based on their physical appearance, which contributes to their reluctance to turn on cameras.
  - This gendered difference in perception can influence both student engagement and the overall classroom dynamic in virtual learning.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Black, Indigenous, and people of color who identify as women feared being judged based on their physical appearance and consequently rarely chose to turn on their cameras."

### Implications for Online Learning and Identity Formation
- **Engagement and Authenticity:**
  - The reluctance to reveal one’s physical space due to discomfort with virtual backgrounds can reduce the richness of social interactions in online classes.
  - This diminished self-expression may hinder the development of a positive, engaged learning environment.
  
- **Recommendations for Improvement:**
  - Universities and instructors are encouraged to better align technology use with students' identity needs.
  - Possible strategies include:
    - Training instructors on identity personalization techniques.
    - Incorporating identity-friendly features that invite users to customize their online presence.
    - Considering alternative or additional virtual background options that balance privacy and authenticity.

*Summary:*  
"Zoom Affordances and Identity: A Case Study" shows that while Zoom is designed to be familiar and user-friendly, its virtual background functionality inadvertently suppresses identity expression. Students, especially those from marginalized backgrounds, feel uneasy exposing their personal environments, which affects their engagement and how they are perceived. The study highlights a need for digital learning environments to offer better identity affordances to support both personal expression and academic engagement.
