# How Time Distorts Our Sense of Culture

*November 15, 2025*

---

Growing up in Kolkata and Dhanbad, the Sunday breakfast ritual begins at dawn in my mother's kitchen. The smell of mustard oil (shorsher tel) heating in the kadai reaches my bedroom first—sharp, unmistakable. By the time I shuffle downstairs, she's already rolling out the dough for luchi, those golden puffed discs that will emerge from the oil like small miracles, their surfaces blistered and gleaming.

The shada torkari simmers in another pot—potatoes and bengal gram transformed by minimal spices into something that rises above their humble origins. Steam rises in the early light, carrying with it the smell of nigella seeds tempering in ghee. This is Bengali culture distilled into a single meal: the luchi's delicate crackle as you tear it open, the way the white curry pools in the center. We crowd around the table, our fingers sticky with oil, competing for the best pieces while the adults drink cha.

This breakfast feels ancient to me. Timeless. The kind of tradition that stretches back through my grandmother's grandmother's grandmother, an unbroken chain connecting us to something eternal about being Bengali.

### Disillusionment

Except the potatoes—the centerpiece of this "timeless" meal—only arrived in Bengal roughly two hundred years ago.

I learned this fact fairly recently. Bengali culture is millennia old. The potato is younger than the United States. How could something so central to my sense of cultural identity be so... recent?

Memory is a poor historian; a few generations feel like forever.

This cognitive dissonance sent me down a rabbit hole that reshaped how I understand culture itself. Because it turns out, the foods we consider most "authentic" are often historical newcomers, arrivals that slipped into tradition so seamlessly that we forgot they came from elsewhere.

Once I noticed the potato's short history, I started seeing the same time-trick everywhere.

### Discovery

In Naples, a grandmother moves through her kitchen with the practiced economy of someone who's made this sauce ten thousand times. Tomatoes—San Marzanos, naturally—glisten in the pot, their skins splitting to release sweet red pulp. Basil leaves rest on the cutting board, waiting for their moment. The smell of garlic sizzling in olive oil fills the narrow street outside her window, mingling with the sounds of Vespa engines and the shouts of children playing calcio.

This seems like the essence of Italian cooking, doesn't it? UNESCO-listed Mediterranean heritage.

But as food historian David Gentilcore documents, tomatoes only became central to Italian cuisine in the late 19th century—roughly when my great-grandparents were born. They arrived from the Americas in the 16th century but were considered decorative, possibly poisonous. The iconic red sauce of Italian-American restaurants, the base of pizza margherita, is younger than the telephone.

---

The time-trick shows up again.

In Chengdu, a chef at a local restaurant adjusts the heat under his wok. Dried chilies—hundreds of them—wait in neat piles, ready to transform into the aggressive, numbing heat that defines Sichuan food. The ma-la sensation, that electric tingle on the lips and tongue, feels primal. This assault of flavor makes you sweat and reach for more rice even as your mouth protests.

But Brian Dott's research reveals that chilies only arrived in Sichuan in the 17th or 18th century, part of the Columbian Exchange that remapped global cuisines. Before that, Sichuan food used aromatics and Sichuan peppercorns for numbing spice, but not the fiery capsaicin burn we now consider essential. The transformation happened so completely that it's hard to imagine Sichuan food without chilies—yet for most of its history, that's exactly what it was.

---

Different city, same illusion.

A Tokyo office worker slides his tray along the cafeteria line. The curry rice arrives in its familiar form: amber sauce ladled over white rice, a few chunks of potato and carrot visible in the thick gravy. The ceramic plate is clean, the portions measured. He carries it to a table by the window and eats efficiently, the mild comfort of the curry providing exactly the warmth needed on a gray afternoon.

Curry feels quintessentially Japanese now—comfort food served everywhere from school lunches to convenience stores. But Katarzyna Cwiertka's scholarship shows it only entered Japan in the 1870s, brought by the British Navy and adapted by the Japanese military as a way to modernize and Westernize. It's been "Japanese" for roughly 150 years—a tiny sliver of Japan's multi-millennium history, yet it occupies enormous emotional real estate in the national cuisine.

---

Different continent, same pattern.

In a Tehran café, men gather around small tables, their conversations punctuated by the clink of glass cups—estekan—placed carefully back on saucers. The tea inside is amber-clear, sweetened with sugar cubes held between the teeth as they sip. Steam rises, carrying the fragrance of strong black tea blended with local traditions. The ritual of tea drinking seems inseparable from Persian culture, from the hospitality codes that govern these gatherings.

Yet as Rudi Matthee has documented, tea only became central to Iranian life in the late 19th century. For centuries, coffee held that social position. Tea arrived through trade with Russia and British India, then was domestically cultivated. Within two generations, it had displaced coffee so thoroughly that most Iranians now think of tea as their ancient national drink.

---

And again.

Cairo at sunset, and the koshari carts emerge on every corner. A vendor assembles the dish with practiced precision: layers of rice, lentils, macaroni, topped with crispy fried onions and tangy tomato sauce. It's cheap, filling, vegetarian by default. The crunch of those onions against the soft carbs beneath creates a texture symphony that feels fundamentally Egyptian.

But Projit Mukharji's work traces koshari's origins to the late 19th or early 20th century, a hybrid born from Egypt's colonial encounters—Italian pasta, Indian rice and lentils, Egyptian execution. It became the national dish within living memory, yet it now represents Egyptian street food culture so completely that its composite origins have been forgotten.

---

The pattern repeats.

In Mexico City, the trompo rotates under heat lamps, layers of marinated pork stacked high with a crown of pineapple on top. The taquero shaves off thin strips with practiced knife work, catching them on a small corn tortilla. The meat sizzles as it hits the surface, achiote-red and glistening. The smell alone—pork fat, pineapple sweetness, charred edges—draws a line of customers that stretches down the block.

Al pastor tacos feel deeply Mexican, a street food that embodies Mexico City's vibrant, chaotic energy. But Jeffrey Pilcher's research shows this dish only emerged in the mid-20th century, created by Lebanese immigrants adapting their shawarma techniques to Mexican ingredients. It's been "traditional" for maybe seventy years—less than a human lifetime—yet it's now considered an essential part of Mexican culinary identity.

---

One more time.

Back in India, at every railway station platform, chai wallahs pour tea in high arcs from one metal vessel to another, aerating the milky liquid until it's perfectly frothy. The smell of cardamom and cloves cuts through diesel fumes and crowd noise. This chai—sweet, spiced, milky—is the social glue of India, served at every gathering, every business meeting, every family event.

But Lizzie Collingham's work demonstrates that this specific preparation—what we now call masala chai—only became widespread in the 20th century, promoted by the Indian Tea Association to create a domestic market for tea previously grown only for export. The spices are Indian, yes, but the combination and the ritual are remarkably recent.

---

Seven cuisines. Seven identities. All transformed in the blink of historical time.

What's happening here reveals a deeper pattern: cultures don't preserve static traditions—they continuously adapt and reframe innovations as heritage. The tomato becomes "essentially Italian," chilies become "fundamentally Sichuan," and curry becomes "quintessentially Japanese," all within a handful of generations. Recency is not inauthenticity; adaptation is the feature that keeps traditions breathing.

### Synthesis

This isn't about authenticity versus fakery. It's about how human memory compresses time, how a few generations can feel like forever, how the brain mistakes familiarity for antiquity. As historians Eric Hobsbawm and Terence Ranger explored in *The Invention of Tradition*, many practices we consider ancient were actually formalized quite recently, then projected backward to create a sense of continuity.

In software engineering, we inherit legacy defaults and invisible conventions, assuming they've always existed in their current form. Culture works similarly: we inherit systems—food traditions, rituals, identity markers—without questioning the dependency chain. Like engineering cultures, cuisines quietly adopt new defaults; after a couple versions, yesterday's novelty reads as the "standard library."

The Columbian Exchange, which Alfred Crosby documented, was history's largest platform migration—foods, animals, diseases, and people moving between hemispheres, rewriting every culture they touched. But unlike a software migration, this happened before version control, before changelogs. The changes integrated so smoothly that we lost track of what changed and when.

In engineering and team culture, we see the same pattern. When someone says "we've always done it this way," they often mean 2-3 generations of engineers—perhaps 8-10 years in tech time. The practice feels foundational, but it's actually a recent adaptation that solved a specific problem and then calcified into unquestioned process. Culture is a running system, not a static codebase. What we call "tradition" is often just version 3.7.2, not 1.0.0—and that's okay.

This matters because the stories we tell about our cultures shape our politics, our borders, our definitions of who belongs. When we think our traditions are ancient and unchanging, we become rigid, defensive. We police boundaries that were actually always porous. We reject innovations that might, in two generations, feel just as "authentic" as the potato curry on my mother's table.

---

### Empowerment

Sunday morning, and I'm back at that breakfast table. I slide another luchi onto my plate, and I tear it open to let the shada torkari pool in the center. The potato is still the star, pale and perfect.

Now I see this meal differently. Not as a static relic of ancient tradition, but as evidence of something more interesting: Bengali culture's genius for adaptation, for taking what arrived by accident or commerce or conquest and transforming it into something undeniably ours. The potato came from the Andes via colonial trade routes, but what we did with it—the specific alchemy of spices and technique—that's the culture. The creativity lives in the transformation, not in claims of timeless purity.

Every culture does this. The ability to absorb, adapt, and claim new elements as "ours" isn't a bug—it's the feature that keeps cultures alive across centuries. Stagnant traditions die. Living ones eat potatoes for breakfast and call it heritage.

So here's my question for you: What "ancient" tradition in your culture is actually younger than your grandparents? What have we collectively forgotten arrived recently, because it integrated so seamlessly that it now feels eternal?

At work, try this: pick one "we've always done it this way" practice older than five years and ask when, why, and by whom it was introduced. The answer might surprise you—and might change how you think about what your culture can become next.
