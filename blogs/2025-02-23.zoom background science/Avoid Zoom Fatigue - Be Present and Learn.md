# Avoid Zoom Fatigue, Be Present and Learn
**Authors:** Erik Peper, Vietta Wilson, Marc Martin, Erik Rosegard, and Richard Harvey  
**Publication Date:** 2021

## Abstract
- **Overview:** The paper examines why students find synchronous online Zoom classes challenging—reporting issues with attention, isolation, and increased anxiety—and offers strategies to optimize learning.
- **Key Findings:** Approximately 80% of 350 college students reported difficulty staying focused during Zoom classes.
- **Strategies Proposed:** Adjusting one’s physical setup, actively engaging nonverbally, reducing multitasking, and using methods to alleviate vision and posture strain.

## Introduction
- **Context:** The rapid transition from in-person to online teaching during COVID‑19 forced educators and students to rely on Zoom for synchronous learning.
- **Challenges Identified:** Students struggle with divided attention due to digital distractions (e.g., smartphones, emails, social media) and the lack of natural nonverbal feedback, which in turn impacts academic achievement.
- **Objective:** To explore the reasons behind these challenges and to propose practical strategies for students to remain present and enhance learning in an online environment.

## Communication and Attention in Online Learning
- **Digital Distractions:** Constant notifications and the ease of multitasking (referred to as “semitasking”) result in reduced focus and lower quality of learning.
- **Lack of Nonverbal Feedback:** In Zoom classes, the diminished visibility of facial expressions and body language hinders natural communication and makes it difficult for instructors to gauge student engagement.
- **Impact on Learning:** Reduced nonverbal cues and increased distractions lead to poorer memory retention and lower academic performance.

## Physical and Visual Strain
- **Near Vision Stress:** Continuous screen time forces the eyes to focus on a near object, causing muscle tension and visual fatigue.
- **Postural Issues:** Prolonged sitting, often in a slouched position, decreases energy levels and may contribute to physical discomfort, further impeding concentration.
- **Physical Inactivity:** The reduced physical activity associated with staying indoors can negatively affect both mental and physical well-being.

## Strategies to Optimize Learning and Reduce Zoom Fatigue
### Be Present to Learn
- **Optimize Camera Setup:**
  - Ensure proper lighting so your face is clearly visible.
  - Position the camera at eye level and look directly at it to simulate face-to-face interaction.
- **Active Engagement:**
  - Use deliberate facial expressions and body language (e.g., nodding, smiling) even if you are not speaking.
  - Treat the online class as if you were in a one-on-one conversation to boost engagement.

### Configure Your Environment
- **Task-Specific Environments:**
  - Create distinct settings for different activities (e.g., studying, working, socializing).
  - Use specific cues such as background images, lighting, or even attire (like a “study T-shirt”) to signal the beginning of a focused session.
- **Minimize Distractions:**
  - Turn off notifications for emails, texts, and social media during class.
  - Establish structured time blocks dedicated solely to learning.

### Optimize Arousal and Regenerate Vision
- **Take Regular Movement Breaks:**
  - Stand up and stretch or perform short physical exercises (e.g., dancing, skipping) every 30 minutes to boost energy and reduce physical strain.
- **Regenerate Vision:**
  - Practice techniques such as “palming” (cover your eyes with your hands for a few minutes) to relax eye muscles.
  - Periodically look at distant objects (e.g., clouds, trees) to ease near vision stress.

## Conclusion
- **Summary:** The paper outlines that while online synchronous learning via Zoom poses significant challenges—including digital distractions, lack of nonverbal cues, and physical strain—there are actionable strategies to combat these issues.
- **Key Takeaway:** By adjusting their physical setup, actively engaging during class, minimizing multitasking, and incorporating regular movement and vision-restoration breaks, students can reduce Zoom fatigue and improve their learning outcomes.
- **Final Thought:** Both instructors and students share responsibility in creating an environment conducive to active learning; by adopting these strategies, one can “be present” and enhance both engagement and academic performance in an online setting.


## Key Information on Video Conferencing Backgrounds and Their Impact on Perception

### Overview
- **Objective:**  
  The paper examines why students find online synchronous Zoom classes more challenging, linking issues such as distractions, multitasking, and lack of nonverbal cues to reduced focus and increased fatigue. It then proposes strategies to optimize learning and improve presence during online sessions.
- **Context:**  
  The study was motivated by reports of increased isolation, anxiety, and difficulty maintaining attention during long Zoom sessions—factors that detract from effective learning.

### The Role of the Physical Environment and Background
- **Impact on Self-Presentation:**  
  The authors note that the visual environment, including the background seen during a video conference, plays a key role in forming impressions. The background, together with one’s facial expressions and body language, contributes to the overall impression the speaker creates.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Be aware that you and your background together create an impression. Does the camera show you engaged, distracted, or lying in bed?"
- **Background as a Source of Distraction:**  
  The paper highlights that visual distractions from one’s environment (e.g., cluttered or uninspiring backgrounds) can compound the challenge of maintaining attention. This distracts both the presenter and the audience, reducing the quality of interaction and learning.
  
### Strategies to Optimize the Visual Environment
- **Camera and Lighting Configuration:**  
  The authors advise configuring one’s camera setup so that the face is clearly visible. Proper lighting and an appropriate background are essential to present a positive and engaged image.
- **Creating Task-Specific Environments:**  
  Students are encouraged to “configure their body and environment” by arranging the physical space to support focus. This includes choosing or creating a background that is free of distractions and that conveys professionalism.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Arrange your workspace so that your background—together with your on-camera appearance—sends the right message about your engagement and professionalism."
  
### Broader Implications for Learning and Presence
- **Reducing Multitasking and Digital Distractions:**  
  The paper suggests that one way to combat Zoom fatigue is to reduce distractions (both digital and environmental). By eliminating unnecessary notifications and ensuring that the background does not pull attention away, learners can maintain a higher level of focus.
- **Optimizing Arousal and Visual Rest:**  
  Recommendations include taking regular breaks to move and to look away from the screen, thus reducing the near-vision stress that is exacerbated by a static background and prolonged screen exposure.

### Summary
- The paper argues that the background seen in a Zoom meeting is not merely a passive element—it interacts with the speaker’s presentation and influences how the audience perceives and engages with the session.
- By paying attention to the visual cues (including background quality and composition), both instructors and students can mitigate the negative impacts of prolonged video conferencing.
- **Overall Notable Insight:**  
  The combined effect of one’s background and on-camera behavior forms a “digital presence” that can either enhance or detract from communication effectiveness and learning outcomes.
