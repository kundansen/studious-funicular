# Student Impressions of Instructors Based on Zoom Backgrounds: Investigating Perceived Technology Skills of Instructors and Affective Learning of Students
**Authors:** Stephanie Kelly, Jihyun Kim, Ian Berry, Ryan Goke  
**Publication Date:** May 03, 2024

## 1. Introduction
- **Context:** The rapid shift to distance learning during COVID‑19 forced many educators to adopt video conferencing (e.g., Zoom). 
- **Issue:** Beyond content delivery, questions arise about how instructors’ use of Zoom backgrounds affects students’ perceptions and learning.
- **Purpose:** This study examines the impact of different Zoom background choices on:
  - Students’ judgments of instructors’ technology use skills.
  - Students’ affective learning (i.e., their emotional responses to the instructor and course content).

### 1.1 Instructional Beliefs Model (IBM)
- **Overview:** IBM posits that student learning is indirectly influenced by student characteristics, classroom characteristics, and instructor behaviors.
- **Relevance:** Although IBM was developed before Zoom, this study argues that an instructor’s Zoom background can be viewed as a classroom characteristic or an aspect of instructor behavior that shapes student beliefs.

### 1.2 Instructor Zoom Backgrounds
- **Role in Identity:** Backgrounds serve as a visual element of the “classroom” and may signal either professionalism or personal style.
- **Technical Skill Indicator:** Some backgrounds require more technical proficiency (e.g., creating a branded background) compared to simpler options (e.g., using Zoom’s blur filter).
- **Implication:** The choice of background may influence how students perceive an instructor’s competence and professionalism.

### 1.3 Student Gender
- **Student Characteristic:** Gender is considered a stable student characteristic.
- **Prior Findings:** Past research shows that gender influences how students perceive technology use; for example, male and female students may judge instructor competence differently.
- **Focus:** This study examines whether student gender moderates the effects of Zoom background choices on perceptions and affective learning.

### 1.4 Attitudes Toward Instructor Technology Competence
- **Perception Focus:** Students form judgments about instructors’ technology skills based on visible cues.
- **Measurement:** The study assesses these attitudes via semantic differential scales.
- **Expectation:** Backgrounds that appear more “professional” or technically skilled may lead to higher ratings, though differences may emerge between male and female students.

### 1.5 Affective Learning
- **Definition:** Affective learning encompasses students’ emotional responses toward an instructor and the course content.
- **Link to Technology:** Effective use of technology can increase feelings of connection and engagement.
- **Hypothesis:** Variations in Zoom background (and by extension, perceived technology competence) may influence affective learning outcomes.

## 2. Materials and Methods
### 2.1 Design and Materials
- **Experimental Design:** A 4 (Zoom background type) x 2 (student gender) between-subjects design.
- **Background Types:** 
  - University branded (used by faculty)
  - Winter landscape (photograph)
  - Blurry (Zoom’s default blur filter)
  - Office (actual office background)
- **Lecture Videos:** Created on a green screen with two instructors (one male, one female) delivering identical scripted content.

### 2.2 Procedure
- **Recruitment:** Undergraduate students were invited via university channels.
- **Viewing:** Participants watched a lecture video (with a fixed duration to ensure full viewing) randomly assigned to one of eight conditions.
- **Survey:** After the video, participants responded to items assessing:
  - Their affective learning for both the instructor and the content.
  - Their attitudes toward the instructor’s technology use skills.
- **Follow-up:** Demographic questions were included after the main survey.

### 2.3 Sample
- **Participants:** 576 undergraduate students, with a majority of female respondents.
- **Demographics:** Diverse racial/ethnic backgrounds and class standings.
- **Assignment:** Participants were randomly distributed across the eight video conditions (each combination of background type and instructor gender).

### 2.4 Measures
- **Affective Learning:** Assessed separately for the instructor and for course content using McCroskey’s scales.
- **Instructor Technology Competence:** Measured with semantic differential items.
- **Control Variable:** Predicted future usage of the lecture content was included.

## 3. Results
- **Main Effects:**
  - **Student Gender:** Female students generally rated instructors’ technology skills and affective learning more favorably than male students.
  - **Zoom Backgrounds:** No main effect of background type was observed overall; however, certain backgrounds (e.g., the blurry background) notably influenced male students’ ratings.
- **Interaction Effects:**
  - For affective learning and perceptions of technology skills, differences emerged in specific background conditions:
    - In the blurry background condition, male students rated instructors’ technology skills lower and showed less positive affect.
    - In contrast, the winter background was consistently rated well by both genders.
- **Statistical Controls:** Instructor sex and predicted future usage were controlled in the analyses.

## 4. Discussion
- **Interpretation:** 
  - The findings suggest that while Zoom’s interface is deemed “user-friendly,” background choices can subtly influence students’ perceptions of instructor competence and their emotional connection to the content.
  - Male students, in particular, seem more sensitive to certain background types (e.g., blurry), which may distract from content and lower technology competence ratings.
- **Implications:** 
  - Instructors should consider using personalized or professional backgrounds that align with course objectives.
  - The study expands the IBM by integrating technology use as a factor in student learning outcomes.
- **Notable Finding:** The only background that received uniformly positive responses was the winter background, possibly due to its neutral and aesthetically pleasing nature.

### 4.1 Limitations and Future Research Directions
- **Sample Limitations:** Lack of diversity in gender (no third-gender participants) and recruitment from communication courses only.
- **Temporal Effects:** The study captures only initial reactions; longitudinal research is needed to examine changes over time.
- **Background Variety:** Future studies should explore a wider range of background types, including more personalized options.
- **Experimental Setting:** The controlled nature of the experiment might not fully capture the dynamics of real classroom settings.

### 4.2 Conclusion
- **Summary:** Instructors’ Zoom background choices affect student perceptions of technology skills and affective learning—particularly among male students.
- **Recommendation:** For optimal student impressions and learning outcomes, instructors should consider using uploaded, well-designed virtual backgrounds that signal technical proficiency and professionalism.
- **Broader Implications:** These findings highlight the importance of aligning technological presentation with instructional goals to support both identity and learning in virtual environments.

## Key Information on Zoom Backgrounds and Their Effects

### Overview
- **Objective:**  
  The study examines how different Zoom backgrounds used by instructors influence students’ perceptions of the instructors’ technology skills and their affective learning experiences.
- **Theoretical Framework:**  
  The research is based on the Instructional Beliefs Model (IBM), which categorizes classroom variables into student characteristics, classroom characteristics, and instructor behaviors.
- **Design:**  
  A 4 (Zoom background: university branded, winter, blurry, office) × 2 (student gender) between-subjects experimental design was used. Lecture videos with controlled content and varied background types were shown to students, who then rated the instructor’s technology competence and affective learning outcomes.

### Impact of Zoom Backgrounds on Perception and Judgment

#### Background as an Indicator of Professionalism and Competence
- **Signal of Professionalism:**  
  The type of Zoom background can serve as a nonverbal cue indicating an instructor’s level of professionalism and technical adeptness. For example, university-branded and office backgrounds tend to convey higher competence.
- **Negative Impact of Certain Backgrounds:**  
  The “blurry” background, in particular, was associated with lower ratings among male students regarding both the instructor’s technology skills and the quality of course content.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Male students in the blurry background condition rated the instructor’s technology use skills more negatively than female students."

#### Location Appearance and Privacy Considerations
- **Personal Environment Exposure:**  
  Some students expressed discomfort with instructors revealing their personal spaces. Concerns were especially pronounced among students from lower-income backgrounds.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "I don’t like that my professors and peers can see my room as it’s supposed to be a private place."
- **Effect on Learning:**  
  Backgrounds that reveal personal environments may detract from the professional image of the instructor, thereby negatively influencing student affective learning.

#### Influence on Affective Learning
- **Differential Impact:**  
  While backgrounds like the winter scene received relatively favorable ratings from both male and female students, other backgrounds (e.g., blurry and office) resulted in lower affective learning ratings, particularly among male students.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Female students rated the instructor more favorably in the university branded and office background conditions, while male students’ ratings varied significantly based on the background."
- **Overall Outcome:**  
  The study suggests that the background choice can subtly shift student engagement and their emotional connection to the course content.

### Gender Differences in Perception
- **General Trends:**  
  Female students tend to rate instructor technology skills and affective learning more positively across all background types compared to male students.
- **Interaction Effects:**  
  The effect of Zoom backgrounds is more pronounced among male students, who seem more sensitive to the nuances of background choice.
  - **Quote:**  
    > "Female students consistently rated the instructor favorably across Zoom background types, whereas male students reported different ratings based on background choice."

### Implications and Recommendations
- **For Instructors:**  
  - Select Zoom backgrounds that reinforce a professional image and signal technological competence.
  - Consider personalized or neutral backgrounds that balance privacy with professionalism.
- **For Educational Institutions:**  
  - Develop guidelines or training for instructors on virtual identity presentation.
  - Encourage practices that help optimize student engagement and reduce negative perceptions associated with less formal or distracting backgrounds.

*Summary:*  
The paper illustrates that Zoom backgrounds significantly affect how students perceive their instructors—impacting both judgments of technological competence and affective learning. It reveals gender differences in these perceptions, with male students being more negatively influenced by certain background choices (e.g., blurry backgrounds). The study recommends that both instructors and institutions should consider these factors when designing and implementing online learning environments.
