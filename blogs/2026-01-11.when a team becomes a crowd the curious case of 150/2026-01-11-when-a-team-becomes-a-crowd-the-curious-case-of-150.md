### Where the Number Comes From, and Why It Isn't a Law

Gladwell popularized this idea in *The Tipping Point*, drawing on the work of anthropologist Robin Dunbar. Dunbar argued that humans have a cognitive limit to the number of stable social relationships they can maintain. He suggested this 150-max as a way to notice when a group of humans still behaves like a community, and when it has quietly turned into a crowd.

Dunbar arrived at the number by studying primates, correlating neocortex size with average group size, and then extrapolating to humans. The result hovered around 148, which most people round up for convenience. Dunbar was careful not to treat this as a hard ceiling. The confidence range is wide, and a number of factors — culture, geography, the kind of work being done — affect the outcome.

Dunbar offered a surprisingly human way to explain it. It's the number of people you wouldn't feel awkward joining uninvited for a drink if you ran into them at a bar. That framing shifts the conversation away from org charts and headcounts, and toward familiarity and shared context.

Later research used more mechanical language to convey the same idea. Social relationships consume cognitive processing power. As groups grow, coordination costs don't increase gradually. They compound. At a certain point, better tools stop helping, and structural change becomes unavoidable.

### Why Smaller Groups Learn Faster

Inside companies, people often say small teams move faster. They cite small garage-bound startups as examples. That's only partly true. What actually makes them faster is that trust is denser and easier to maintain.

Two mechanisms explain why small, dense teams learn, develop, and deliver faster. First, attention is finite: every relationship consumes time and cognitive effort, and as the roster grows, that attention thins and coordination frays. Second, expertise is distributed: when you can quickly map who knows what, problems get solved faster.

In groups that stay below roughly 150 people, attention still flows in recognizable ways. People know one another by name, not just by role or title. Questions don't need elaborate framing. Help shows up before frustration sets in. People understand each other and settle into an interpersonal dynamic.

In tightly connected teams, fixes, questions, and tiny process changes reach the people who need them quickly; the network itself does the work. Gladwell also stresses the uneven social leverage of a few key people:

"*The success of any kind of social movement is heavily dependent on the involvement of people with a particular and rare set of social gifts.*"

In practice, that means small teams with strong connectors, visible rituals, and a short feedback cadence will absorb and amplify good practices far faster than a dispersed, bureaucratic group.

Put those together, and the result is empirical: fewer pointless meetings, shorter feedback loops, and more predictable outputs. The practical question becomes not how many people you have on paper, but how much useful attention circulates between them.

### The Startup That Lives Inside Every Large Company

A small group inside a big company behaves like a startup. Work moves quickly, arguments are sharper and more productive, and people feel a sense of ownership that goes beyond their job description. This is what Agile refers to as the storming, norming, and performing stages.

Then the group grows, and slowly, almost imperceptibly, that feeling fades.

This, per Gladwell, is a scaling problem.

Gladwell often pointed to W. L. Gore as an example of taking this constraint seriously. The company deliberately limited the size of its facilities. When groups became too large, they split. Not because they were failing, but because they were succeeding. Beyond a certain size, coordination friction increased, ownership blurred, and informal norms lost their force.

The lesson isn't to stay small forever. It's to scale by replication instead of aggregation. Build multiple small engines rather than one massive one. Gladwell uses the example of Gore-Tex creating a new office location whenever its 150-spot parking lot becomes too small for the people working there.

### Why Tools Rarely Fix Social Scaling Problems

It's tempting to believe that better tools will solve these issues. Our first reaction is usually to add more tools — more dashboards, more Teams channels, more documentation, more design diagrams, and more tooling to support human-to-human communication.

Tools do work to some extent — but then they stop scaling. Tools increase the number of ways information can move, but they don't increase attention. In many cases, they spread it thinner. Malcolm's chapter in The Tipping Point was the first time I was reading about the math and science behind why this failure happens.

What makes a difference are simple, easy-to-follow rituals. Consider a quick weekly demo, partnering with a reliable onboarding buddy, and maintaining clear ownership that stays consistent throughout the year. These small practices help make information more accessible by externalizing context, so it's not just stored in people's heads.

When onboarding takes longer, meetings start to pile up, or basic decisions seem to require multiple rounds of alignment, it's usually a sign that social bandwidth is stretched. That's often a good moment to think about splitting the team into two, which can bring a much-needed boost of energy and clarity.
