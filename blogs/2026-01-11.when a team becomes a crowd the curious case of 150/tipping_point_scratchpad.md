## Key claims to support
1. Dunbar’s ~150 is a useful heuristic (mean ≈148; CI wide ~100–230) — not a law. Source: Dunbar 1992/2010. 
2. The mechanism is cognitive & social (neocortex size, transactive memory, gossip/repeated interaction). Sources: Dunbar papers, Dávid‑Barrett & Dunbar 2013.
3. In corporate contexts, tie strength (daily collaborators vs. acquaintances) matters more than raw headcount — leaders can shape attention and onboarding rituals to maintain trust and speed.
4. Digital tools change channel frequency, not the underlying attention budget.
5. Practical experiments (30/90/365) can test whether small nodes preserve velocity and knowledge transfer.

## Critiques & caveats to include (brief)
- Different methods produce different estimates (Bernard–Killworth studies show higher numbers). Use as nuance: measure, don’t worship the number.
- Cultural and task differences: distributed teams and highly modular tasks may violate the heuristics.
- Measurement problems: "close" is fuzzy — propose operational metrics (interaction frequency, help requests, number of people who can reasonably fix a bug).

---

## Corporate‑focused implications (notes)
- Team size ≠ relationship density; leaders should focus on: attention allocation, onboarding rituals, and connectors.
- Small autonomous pods maintain momentum because: low coordination overhead, fast feedback, high visibility of outcomes (short loops).
- Signals to preserve: demo cadence, buddy onboarding, cross‑team swap, short Loom demos for async transfer, weekly curated digest.

---

## Practical experiments (operationalized)
- 30‑day experiment: start a 15‑minute weekly demo, assign onboarding buddy, measure: time‑to‑first‑merge, new‑hire NPS after 30 days.
- 90‑day experiment: run a cross‑team swap (one person for two weeks), measure knowledge transfer (paired sessions completed, number of bugs fixed by the swap person without help).
- 365‑day assessment: decide replicate/split/federate based on onboarding cost per hire, product velocity (feature throughput), and meeting hours per person.

Metrics (heuristics): time‑to‑productivity, meeting hours per person, time‑to‑fix, percentage of team that can push to production without help, new‑hire satisfaction.
