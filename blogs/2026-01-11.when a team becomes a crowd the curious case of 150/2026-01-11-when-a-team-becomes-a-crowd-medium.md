# When a Team Becomes a Crowd: The Curious Case of 150

*January 11, 2026*

*Published on Medium: https://medium.com/@kundansen/when-a-team-becomes-a-crowd-the-curious-case-of-150-a452396f2456*

## Why Groups Larger Than 150 People Stop Working

There's a number that keeps showing up in organizational design: 150.

It appears in the military (squad size). It appears in corporate structures (the size at which communication suddenly breaks down). It appears in anthropology. And for some reason, most of us ignore it entirely when we're building teams.

This is Robin Dunbar's number—a cognitive limit on stable social relationships that Malcolm Gladwell popularized in *The Tipping Point*. And it explains more about why your organization feels dysfunctional than you might think.

## How We Got the Number

Dunbar, an anthropologist, derived this by studying primate neocortex sizes and extrapolating to humans. He wasn't looking for a magic number. He was trying to answer a question: how big can a stable group get before the cognitive load of maintaining relationships breaks down?

The number itself is somewhat arbitrary—it's 150 give or take—but the mechanism is real. The neocortex, the part of your brain that handles social cognition, has limits. It can track a certain number of relationships simultaneously. Beyond that, something breaks.

Dunbar defined it practically: "the number of people you wouldn't feel awkward joining for a drink uninvited at a bar." It's not about formal hierarchy. It's about familiarity.

## What Changes at 150?

Below 150, several things work naturally:

**Knowing people by name.** You walk into a room and you recognize faces. You know what these people do. You know their relationships to each other. This knowledge is free—it's just ambient awareness from working together.

**Questions have context.** When someone asks for help, you already know enough about them to contextualize the question. You know who they work with, what they've tried before, what their constraints are. You don't need a Slack thread explaining their history.

**Informal networks distribute solutions.** When someone has a problem, the solution-finding happens through natural networks. You ask Sarah, who asks Mike, who knows the right person. Information flows naturally because the graph of relationships is dense enough to have shortcuts.

**Trust is default.** In smaller groups, you trust people because you've been around them. You've seen how they handle problems. You know their reputation firsthand, not through rumor.

At 150, these things still mostly work. Just above 150, they stop.

## What Breaks

Once you cross 150, the cognitive load shifts:

**Names become faces become strangers.** You walk into the all-hands and there are people you don't recognize. You don't know what they do. You don't know if they're new or if you've just forgotten them.

**Questions need context.** Now when someone asks for help, you need background. Slack threads become longer. Meetings become more complicated. The shared context that used to be free now requires explicit documentation.

**Informal networks collapse.** The graph of relationships becomes too sparse. You can't find the shortcut. So organizations add coordinators. Then more coordinators. Then they add tools to replace the coordinators.

**Trust becomes policy.** Because you can't know people, you have to trust the process instead. You have to trust that reviews are thorough, that approvals work, that policy is enforced.

## The Implication: Don't Scale Up

The worst response to exceeding 150 is to solve the problem with tools. More dashboards. Better communication platforms. More documentation. Clearer org charts.

None of this works because the problem isn't information flow. The problem is cognition. Your brain can only track so many relationships. You can't add more attention by adding technology.

Some organizations have figured this out. W. L. Gore, the materials science company, has a remarkable policy: when a facility approaches 150 people, they split it. Success triggers division, not consolidation. They've built the entire organization around replicating small units rather than scaling large ones.

It's counterintuitive. You'd think that larger organizations would be more efficient. But below a certain size, the efficiency comes from trust and informal networks, not from process optimization.

## What Actually Works

Instead of tools, small rituals do the heavy lifting:

**Weekly demos.** Everyone sees what everyone else is building. You recognize faces. You understand what work matters. You build ambient awareness without meetings.

**Consistent mentorship.** One person knows the new hire well enough to contextualize their questions. The new hire doesn't have to start from zero context with every question.

**Clear ownership.** Everyone knows who owns what. Not because of org charts, but because you've heard it repeatedly from people you know.

**Distributed decision-making.** Small groups make their own calls. They don't wait for approval from committees that don't know their context.

These aren't scalable solutions. They don't work in organizations of thousands. But they're what make organizations of 50 actually function.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

The uncomfortable truth is that most of us are trying to run organizations that are too big for how humans work. We keep hiring. We keep adding layers. We keep building processes to compensate for the fact that people can't possibly know each other anymore.

It works, after a fashion. You get bureaucracy. You get slower decision-making. You get people who optimize for process rather than outcome. But you're paying the cognitive tax the whole time.

The alternative is smaller organizations connected by clear information flows. Not a flat organization—clear structure matters. But an organization where the default is trust, not verification. Where context is free, not expensive. Where relationships still matter.

If you work in a team of 150 or fewer, pay attention to how things work while they still do. The network effects you're experiencing are real. Once you scale past this, you're solving a different problem entirely.

And if you work in an organization of several thousand, understanding why communication feels broken—it's not a failure of process. It's a reality of cognition.
