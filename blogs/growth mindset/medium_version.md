# Nobody Wants to Be the Tortoise

## Why we all secretly want to be "smarter hares" - and what decades of research reveals about talent, effort, and learning



When we hear the story of the tortoise and the hare, we nod along with the moral - slow and steady wins the race. If we're honest, though, nobody actually wants to be the tortoise.

We want to be a smarter hare. One who's naturally gifted AND works hard. The best of both worlds.

This instinct reveals something deep about how we think about success: we secretly believe talent matters more than anything else. Decades of research - including 40 years of intensive study - tells a very different story. This isn't just about mindset or motivation. It's about fundamentally misunderstanding how learning works and what drives achievement.

## The Smarter Hare Confession

Carol Dweck, a Stanford psychologist who's spent her career studying achievement, noticed something uncomfortable about that fable. When people hear it, they don't want to be the tortoise. They want to be a hare who also tries.

We celebrate "natural talent" constantly. Think about how we talk about successful people: "She's a natural." "He's just gifted." "They have it." We attribute success to inherent qualities, not effort. Our language reveals our beliefs.

It's not just talk, either. Our education system enshrines this view with "gifted and talented" programs that identify and separate children based on early testing. These programs are everywhere - based on the assumption that some kids simply have more innate ability than others.

Dweck calls this a "fixed mindset" - the belief that intelligence and talent are static traits you're born with. In this worldview, success is about proving you're smart. Failure means you're not. If you have to work hard? Well, maybe you're not as gifted as you thought.

## The Talent Obsession

Being labeled "gifted" can actually harm children, and this is where it gets problematic.

In one study, Dweck and her colleagues worked with more than 400 fifth graders. Half were praised for their intelligence: "You must be smart at this!" The other half were praised for their effort: "You must have worked really hard!"

Then they offered the kids a choice: an easy test or a challenging one.

The kids praised for intelligence overwhelmingly chose the easy test. Why risk looking dumb? The kids praised for effort chose the challenging test. They wanted to learn.

When the researchers later gave all the kids a difficult test (that nobody did well on), the "intelligence praise" group fell apart. Their performance dropped. They lost interest. Some even lied about their scores.

The "effort praise" group stayed motivated. Many said they enjoyed the challenging problems. Their performance improved.

The message we send matters enormously. When we praise intelligence, we tell children that success is about looking smart, not getting smarter. We make them fragile. We create what Dweck calls a "fixed mindset" - one that avoids challenge because challenge reveals limitations.

## What the Research Actually Shows

If talent isn't the deciding factor, what drives success?

Benjamin Bloom - a University of Chicago education researcher - spent 40 years studying school learning around the world. His conclusion? Stark and hopeful:

> *"What any person in the world can learn, almost all persons can learn if provided with appropriate prior and current conditions of learning."*

Read that again. Almost all persons can learn what any person can learn. Given the right conditions.

Bloom wasn't theorizing. He studied what happened when students got one-on-one tutoring with immediate feedback. The results were dramatic: average students moved to the 98th percentile. He called this the "[2 sigma problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloom%27s_2_sigma_problem)" - how do we give all students the conditions that produce two standard deviations of improvement (meaning moving from average to exceptional)?

Dweck's longitudinal studies confirm this. Students with a growth mindset - who believe intelligence can be developed - outperform those with a fixed mindset over time. Not immediately. But consistently.

This pattern extends beyond academics. K. Anders Ericsson studied world-class performers across domains - violinists, chess players, athletes. His finding? Expert performance comes from [deliberate practice](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1993-40718-001), not innate talent. Elite violinists had accumulated more than 10,000 hours of focused practice by age 20. The main difference between elite and merely good performers wasn't some magical gift. It was accumulated practice.

Angela Duckworth's research on "[grit](https://www.amazon.com/Grit-Passion-Perseverance-Angela-Duckworth/dp/1501111108)" adds another layer. She found that perseverance and passion for long-term goals predict success better than IQ or talent in contexts from the National Spelling Bee to West Point military academy. She summarizes it in a formula:

**Talent × Effort = Skill**

**Skill × Effort = Achievement**

Effort appears twice in this equation. It builds the skill, and it makes the skill productive. Talent matters. But effort counts twice.

## Your Brain Wants to Change

There's even biological evidence for all this. Your brain isn't fixed - it's plastic.

Neuroscientist Norman Doidge documented case after case of dramatic brain reorganization in "[The Brain That Changes Itself](https://www.amazon.com/Brain-That-Changes-Itself-Frontiers/dp/0143113100)." Stroke victims recovering lost function. Blind people learning to "see" through their tongues. The brain adapts.

You don't need dramatic cases, though. A [2004 study in Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/427311a) looked at people learning to juggle. After three months of practice, MRI scans showed measurable increases in grey matter in brain areas related to visual motion processing. When they stopped practicing, those changes diminished.

Your brain physically responds to what you do. "Neurons that fire together wire together," as neuroscientists say. Practice literally changes your brain structure.

This is the mechanism behind growth mindset. When Dweck talks about developing intelligence, she's not being metaphorical. She's describing neuroplasticity - your brain's ability to reorganize and strengthen connections based on use.

## The Effort-Skill Combination

The synthesis becomes clear: it's not talent versus effort. It's not either-or.

It's effort plus the right conditions.

Duckworth's formula helps clarify this. Talent × Effort = Skill. Effort builds skill through practice and learning. Then Skill × Effort = Achievement. You have to deploy the skill through continued effort.

Think of effort as compound interest. It builds your skill base, and then it makes that skill productive. Effort isn't competing with talent; it's the mechanism by which capability develops and gets deployed.

What are "the right conditions" Bloom mentioned? They include good instruction, timely feedback, sufficient time, challenge matched to current ability, and support when stuck. These aren't nice-to-haves. They're the difference between learning and not learning.

That's why the growth mindset research isn't about "just try harder." It's about creating environments where effort leads to learning. Where challenge is normal, not threatening. Where mistakes are information, not verdicts.

## Beyond the Smarter Hare

We started wanting to be smarter hares - naturally talented and hardworking. We've discovered the question is wrong, though.

The hare lost not because it lacked talent but because it lacked the right conditions and effort. It had the capability but not the application. The tortoise won not by being slow but by being consistent in effort and conditions that supported finishing.

Maybe the real lesson isn't about pacing. It's about the fact that finishing the race matters more than how fast you could theoretically run. Almost anyone can finish if given the right conditions, feedback, and time.

Real-world evidence supports this. A [2019 study published in Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1466-y) examined 12,000+ high school students across the United States. Brief growth mindset interventions - just teaching students that intelligence can be developed - improved grades for lower-achieving students. The effects persisted over time. This isn't theory. It works.

What does this mean practically?

Stop asking "Am I talented enough?" and start asking "What are the conditions I need to learn this?"

Stop celebrating "natural ability" and start noticing accumulated effort and good practice conditions.

Stop labeling children (or yourself) as "gifted" or "not gifted" and start focusing on creating environments where everyone can develop.

We can all be smarter hares - by recognizing that "smart" isn't fixed.

## The Real Moral

Carol Dweck's uncomfortable observation about the tortoise and hare reveals our deepest assumptions about success. Benjamin Bloom's 40 years of research upends them. The neuroscience confirms it. The formula is clearer than we thought: effort builds skill, and effort deploys skill.

That's it.

This matters because every time we celebrate "natural talent," we reinforce a fixed mindset. Every time we say someone is "just gifted," we tell everyone else - including ourselves - that they're not. The research is clear: almost anyone can learn almost anything, given the right conditions.

The smarter hare isn't born. It's built.

Through effort, practice, feedback, and time. Through conditions that support learning instead of sorting. Through believing that capability develops rather than just reveals itself.

So the next time you hear about someone's "natural talent," ask a different question: How many hours of deliberate practice did they put in? What conditions supported their learning? What failures did they learn from?

You might find the tortoise was a hare all along. The hare could have been one too, given better conditions.
