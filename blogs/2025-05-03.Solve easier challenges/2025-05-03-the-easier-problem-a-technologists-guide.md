# The Easier Problem: A Technologist's Guide to Everyday Challenges

*May 3, 2025*

---

Thirty years ago, I hit a wall during a math exam that still haunts my memory. The probability and statistics section was a maze of confusion, and I was lost in it. Not one question seemed approachable with the exam-night-cramming the day before. In a last-ditch effort, I flipped the script: I invented my own questions and solved them, leaving a sheepish note for the professor. Wasn't my fault that none of what I had studied was in the question paper, was it? It was a gamble, but it paid off—I passed. More than that, it revealed a then-bizarre truth I've carried into my career: when a problem feels insurmountable, there's always a simpler starting point if you look for it.

I recently found out that this approach isn't just a quirky story! It's grounded in George Polya's problem-solving framework from *"How to Solve It,"* and it gets even better with Daniel Kahneman's insights into thinking from *"Thinking, Fast and Slow."* Together, they provide a fantastic roadmap for technologists who navigate the daily grind of complex challenges, helping them preserve their sanity and sense of humor along the way!

### What Polya Knew: Start Small, Win Big

George Polya had a knack for cutting through chaos. His advice? "If you can't solve a problem, then there is an easier problem you can solve—find it." It's deceptively simple, but it works. Back in that exam, I didn't know Polya by name, but I was channeling his wisdom. By "tackling" (a.k.a fabricating) questions I could handle, I built a bridge to understanding the mess before me.

For technologists, this translates directly to the workplace. Debugging a spaghetti codebase? Don't stare at the whole beast—zero in on one function and test it. Progress isn't about heroics; it's about momentum. And sometimes, that first small win is all you need to keep going.

### Fast vs. Slow: Kahneman's Two-Speed Mind

Daniel Kahneman's work adds depth to this strategy. He describes two thinking modes: System 1, the fast, gut-driven responder, and System 2, the slower, analytical problem-solver. During my exam meltdown, System 1 was screaming, *You're doomed!* But System 2 stepped in, calmly suggesting, *Let's work with what we've got.*

In tech, we live this tension daily. Deadlines push us toward quick fixes (System 1), but sticky problems—like a mysterious bug or a tricky API integration—demand the deliberate focus of System 2. The trick is knowing when to shift gears. Rushing might feel productive, but slowing down often gets you there faster. Ever tried fixing a glitch in a caffeine-fueled frenzy, only to realize you missed the obvious? Exactly.

### How Does This Help Me Code Better?

Let's get practical. How can you implement these ideas in your next project? When a task seems overwhelming, Polya's strategy of tackling an "easier problem" can be invaluable. For example, if you're developing a new feature and the requirements appear tangled, begin by prototyping a simple element, such as a button that performs one specific action. This may not be the complete solution, but it provides a solid starting point.

Kahneman's lens is a valuable tool that can really make a difference in how we approach problem-solving. Before you dive headfirst into a task, take a moment to pause and think. You might find it helpful to sketch out the logic of the situation or to have a conversation with a teammate who can bring a fresh perspective. Remember, taking this time isn't procrastination; it's like priming your brain, getting it ready to think more clearly and effectively.

And there will be times when you find yourself stuck, feeling like you're banging your head against the monitor in frustration. When that happens, it's essential to step back. Simplify the problem at hand and ask yourself a simple but powerful question: What's the tiniest piece of this puzzle that I can solve right now? More often than not, addressing that small piece can lead you to unlock the bigger, more complex challenge waiting behind it. Just give it a try; you might be surprised at how effective this approach can be!

### Collaboration: Dividing the Complexity

Teams can amplify this approach. A massive project can overwhelm even the sharpest minds, but break it into bite-sized problems, and suddenly it's manageable. Assign each piece to someone's strengths—one person tackles the database, another the UI—and watch the chaos become clear.

I once had the experience of working on an app rollout that was under a lot of pressure with a tight deadline, and things seemed to be falling apart. Instead of panicking together, we decided to break it down: I tackled the backend while a colleague worked on the frontend mockup. We didn't consider ourselves geniuses; we focused on the simpler problems we could solve, like putting on horse blinders to leave out distractions. By the end of it, we had created a working prototype—and an entertaining story to share at the next team happy hour!

### The Takeaway: You've Got This

Complex problems don't always demand extraordinary intelligence. They require the readiness to begin modestly and strategize effectively. Polya provides the approach; Kahneman offers the perspective. Combined, they form a powerful duo for every technologist's toolkit.

Next time you face a problem that seems to taunt you, don't lose hope. Identify the simpler issue, tackle it, and use that as your foundation. While you may not change the course of computing history, you will accomplish your task—and perhaps even enjoy some laughter in the process.
