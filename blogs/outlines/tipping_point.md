# Why ~150 People Is the Sweet Spot for Trust, Training, and Teams

**Metaphor:** Startups inside a ship — small, autonomous engines that keep momentum and learn fast while the larger vessel steers the course.

---

## Opening anecdote (hook)
Full transparency: I barely read non‑fiction until a few years ago, when I started bingeing LinkedIn Learning and O’Reilly. That rabbit hole led me to Malcolm Gladwell—he’s bright, funny, and speaks like a person, not a lecture; if you’re on Long Island, he’s at the Tilles Center in February (Ticketmaster has details).

Quick thesis: In big companies, small teams behave like startups — and the ~150 heuristic matters because it pins a practical boundary for trust, onboarding, and knowledge flow.

---

## 1) Origins & reality check
- Short note on Robin Dunbar and the research behind “Dunbar’s number” (~100–250 range). 
- Malcolm Gladwell popularized it in public writing — useful shorthand, not a law. 
- Why the number is an *approximate* signal, not a hard limit.

## 2) What “close” means in corporate life
- Different tie layers: core collaborators (daily), trusted partners (weekly), reliable acquaintances (~150). 
- How to measure: interaction frequency, dependency, and willingness to ask for help.

## 3) The mechanisms: why small groups learn faster
- Cognitive limits and attention: people can maintain a limited set of stable relationships.
- Transactive memory and gossip: how knowledge moves informally.
- Repeated interaction builds trust, which reduces coordination friction.

## 4) The tiny‑startup model (how pods sustain momentum)
- Small autonomy, tight feedback loops, and rapid experiments.
- Clear signals: who decides, who ships, who fixes.
- Micro‑rituals that keep culture and norms visible.

## 5) Leadership levers that matter
- Connectors: folks who link teams and surface blockers.
- Embedded coaching: short mentorships and onboarding buddies.
- Rituals: weekly show‑and‑tell, demo days, 15‑minute onboarding walkthroughs.

## 6) Scaling patterns: split, federate, or replicate?
- Signs it’s time to split: onboarding load, coordination costs, slowed experiments.
- Federated model: local autonomy + shared standards.
- How to preserve learning across nodes: canonical docs, cross‑pollination swaps.

## 7) Digital reality: Slack, Teams, and the attention tax
- Tools change communication frequency, not attention limits. 
- Bad signals: noisy channels, shallow reactions. 
- Good signals: async demos, short Looms, curated weekly summaries.

## 8) Limits & critiques
- Cross‑cultural and task complexity caveats.
- When Dunbar’s heuristics don’t apply (highly modular tasks, extreme scaling tech). 
- Empirical debates and recent replications/criticisms.

## 9) Practical playbook: 30 / 90 / 365 experiments
- 30 days: run an onboarding checklist, start a weekly 15‑minute demo, assign a buddy. Metrics: time‑to‑first‑merge, new‑hire satisfaction.
- 90 days: pilot federated standards, run a cross‑team swap, measure knowledge transfer (paired sessions completed). 
- 365 days: assess whether nodes should replicate, split, or centralize; measure product velocity and onboarding cost per hire.

## 10) Quick checklist & scripts
- Onboarding script (first 7 days): buddy intro, one pager, first task, demo invite.
- Rituals: 15‑minute weekly demo, monthly demo day, one cross‑team swap per quarter.
- Measurement heuristics: time‑to‑productivity, number of people who can fix a bug, meeting hours per person.
