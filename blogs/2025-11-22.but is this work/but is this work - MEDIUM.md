# When Work Feels Like Play: AI, Autonomy, and the End of Office Hours

## How AI and Always-On Culture Turned My Workday Into a Choose-Your-Own-Adventure Novel

**Tags**: #FutureOfWork, #ArtificialIntelligence, #WorkLifeBalance, #Productivity, #RemoteWork  
**Reading time**: ~12 minutes


## The 8 PM Paradox

It's 8 PM on a weeknight. I'm on my work laptop, tinkering with Sourcegraph Amp, teaching it to refactor my Python scripts. I'm completely absorbed - the kind of deep focus where two hours feel like twenty minutes. My spouse walks past my home office and pauses. "Still working?"

I look up. Am I? And in eight hours, I'll be starting my 4 AM workday.

The code isn't for any specific project. No manager assigned this. No ticket waits for it in JIRA. I'm not logging these hours anywhere. But I'm learning patterns I'll definitely use tomorrow. And honestly? I'm having *fun*. The dopamine hit when Amp nails a tricky refactoring feels like clearing a game level, not completing a work task.

So... is this work? Or is this play that happens to improve my professional capabilities? And why does that question make me feel vaguely guilty either way?

This confusion isn't unique to me. Research on flow states - those moments of total absorption that psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi spent decades studying - shows that [intrinsically motivating activities blur our sense of time and effort](https://www.harpercollins.com/products/flow-mihaly-csikszentmihalyi). When work triggers flow, it stops feeling like "work" in the traditional sense. Add AI tools that provide instant feedback, visible progress, and the thrill of discovery, and you've got a recipe for 11 PM coding sessions that feel more like puzzle-solving than labor.

The [GitHub Copilot research team found](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590) that developers using AI assistants completed tasks 55.8% faster - but more interestingly, they reported higher enjoyment and engagement. The AI didn't just speed up work; it made work feel different. More playful. More exploratory. More... optional?

Which brings us to the heart of the paradox: when work becomes genuinely enjoyable, how do we know where to draw the line? The question itself reveals how blurred the boundaries have become - and suggests we might need a better framework than simply asking "is this work?"


## When All the Old Rules Stopped Working

Once upon a time, "work" was simple to define. Your manager assigned tasks. You showed up at a physical location. You got paid for specific hours. When you left the building, work ended. Clean boundaries. Clear expectations.

Knowledge work complicated that picture decades ago - ideas don't clock out at 5 PM, and your best thinking might happen in the shower - but at least we had some anchors. Most people still went to an office. Collaboration mostly happened in real time. "Work" and "not work" occupied different physical and temporal spaces.

Then remote work demolished the location marker. Then AI demolished the assumption that "if it's enjoyable, it can't be work."

Cal Newport, patron saint of productivity boundaries, has a solution: the shutdown ritual. In [*Deep Work*](https://www.calnewport.com/books/deep-work/) (2016), he describes ending each workday at 5:30 PM sharp with a specific sequence: check email one final time, transfer tomorrow's tasks to your schedule, say "shutdown complete" out loud. The ritual creates psychological closure. Work stays in its box. Life happens outside that box.

Beautiful theory. I tried it.

Day 1, 5:25 PM: Teams message from my teammate in India. 5:28 PM: Remember I promised to review a pull request. 5:30 PM: Stare at laptop screen. 5:31 PM: Feel waves of guilt. 5:32 PM: "Just one quick thing..."

The hard stop lasted exactly 31 minutes.

Here's what Newport's framework - brilliant as it is - struggles to accommodate: modern knowledge work isn't just distributed across time zones. It's distributed across *contexts*. My work happens in Teams, which sits next to WhatsApp on my phone. My "personal development" happens on company-provided O'Reilly subscriptions. My "networking" happens in employee resource groups that feel half-social, half-professional obligation.

Recent research confirms this isn't just me being bad at boundaries. A [2024 Stanford study](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32405) found that 68% of remote/hybrid workers regularly check work messages outside traditional hours, compared to 42% of in-office workers. Microsoft's Work Trend Index reports that asynchronous communication tools and AI assistants have contributed to [changes in when and how work gets done](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index/), making it easier to "just quickly" knock out one more thing.

The old rules didn't just stop working. The entire playing field changed underneath them.


## Welcome to the Gray Zone

So if the old boundaries are gone, what's left? Welcome to the gray zone - that ambiguous middle ground where most modern knowledge workers actually spend our days.

**The Commute Conundrum**: I listen to Cal Newport's audiobook during my train ride to the office. (Yes, I see the irony.) Is that personal development or professional preparation? The [U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics](https://www.bls.gov/tus/) doesn't count commute time as work, but a significant portion of commuters use that time for work-related learning. Should I? Could I expense the audiobook? Does it matter that I'm genuinely enjoying it?

**The ERG Puzzle**: I spend three hours on a Saturday editing photos for our diversity blog and planning next month's speaker series. This is volunteer work for my company's employee resource group. Nobody's paying me. Nobody required it. But it definitely benefits my employer, probably helps my visibility for promotions, and honestly feels more meaningful than some of my actual job tasks. So... work? Not work? Hobby that happens to align with my employer's brand?

**The Learning Labyrinth**: My company gives me unlimited access to LinkedIn Learning and O'Reilly. I spend Sunday afternoon taking a course on prompt engineering because it sounds interesting and might be useful someday. Work? The company paid for it. Not work? I chose to do it on my own time and might never use those skills. Both? Neither?

**The Tool Tinkering**: I mentioned the late-night Amp sessions. Add to that: experimenting with Cursor on weekends, building a personal automation with an AI assistant that I'll definitely adapt for work projects, testing Copilot's new features because I'm curious. Professional development? Hobby coding? Unpaid R&D for my employer?

**The Always-On Everywhere**: My work email lives on my personal phone. Teams notifications appear next to texts from friends. I've messaged people from the grocery store and answered work questions while waiting for my coffee to brew. The tools don't distinguish between contexts, so why should my brain?

**The Blogging Paradox**: Every weekend, I write LinkedIn and Medium blogs about productivity, AI tools, and work culture. This is definitely NOT work - I actually *pay* Medium \$5/month for the privilege of publishing. (Much like my gym membership, both get used roughly once a week and make me feel simultaneously productive and self-indulgent.) Nobody assigns these topics. Nobody compensates the effort. But the posts boost my professional brand, demonstrate thought leadership, and occasionally get cited in conversations with my boss. So... expensive hobby? Career investment? Digital peacocking? All of the above?

These aren't edge cases. According to organizational psychology research, these ambiguous activities fall under "organizational citizenship behaviors" (OCB) - discretionary efforts that support company functioning but fall outside formal job descriptions. The problem? These behaviors are simultaneously praised, expected, unpaid, and supposedly "voluntary." They exist in permanent definitional limbo.

Research on commuting during the pandemic identified this as a "liminal time" problem - the commute provided psychological transition space between home and work, and losing that space during remote work made boundaries even mushier. But now we're living in permanent liminal space, where work and non-work coexist in the same physical location, on the same devices, during the same hours.

This isn't dysfunction. This *is* modern knowledge work.


## The AI Accelerant

If the gray zone was already confusing, AI just turned up the fog machine.

Here's why generative AI makes this problem uniquely worse - or better, depending on your perspective: AI tools tap directly into intrinsic motivation. [Daniel Pink's *Drive*](https://www.danpink.com/books/drive/) (2009) identified three key drivers of intrinsic motivation: autonomy (I control what I do), mastery (I'm getting better at something), and purpose (this matters). AI-assisted work delivers all three in rapid, addictive cycles.

I decide what to try with Amp. (Autonomy.) I see immediate improvement in my code. (Mastery.) The tool helps me solve real problems. (Purpose.) Dopamine floods my brain. Repeat.

Research backs this up. A [2023 MIT study](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adh2586) found that writers using generative AI increased productivity by 37%, but - critically - they also reported higher job satisfaction. The AI didn't just make them faster; it made the work feel more creative and less like drudgery. Other research found the technology blurred the line between "human work" and "AI work" in ways that made tasks more engaging.

This is the paradox: AI makes work feel like *play*. 

Video games have spent decades perfecting engagement mechanics - rapid feedback loops, visible progress bars, achievement unlocks, the thrill of discovery. Agentic AI tools like Cursor, Copilot, and Amp replicate those exact mechanics in professional contexts. Try a prompt. Get instant results. Iterate. Improve. Watch your code transform. Feel the satisfaction.

There I was at 11 PM, teaching Amp to refactor a gnarly legacy function. Got it working. Felt like winning a boss battle. Realized I'd been doing "work" for 90 minutes without noticing. No guilt. No resentment. Just... fun?

Is this what work is *supposed* to feel like? Or have I become that person who "lives to work"? And more disturbingly: does my employer now expect me to spend my evenings playing with AI tools to stay competitive, even though nobody's saying that explicitly?

Self-Determination Theory, the gold-standard framework for understanding motivation, suggests that when activities satisfy our needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness, we experience them as self-determined rather than externally imposed. In other words, when work feels intrinsically motivating, we genuinely *want* to do it. The problem emerges when employers and employees both benefit from that motivation without clearly negotiating whose time and energy is actually being spent.

Research projects that generative AI could automate a significant portion of current work activities by the 2030s. But what we're seeing in early adoption isn't workers doing less - it's workers spending their newly "freed" time managing AI, experimenting with tools, and exploring capabilities. The work doesn't decrease. It just becomes more engaging. And more diffuse. And harder to classify as "on the clock" or "off the clock."


## Why This Actually Matters

At this point, you might be thinking: "So work is fun now and boundaries are fuzzy. Is that really a problem?"

Fair question. And honestly, I'm not sure I have a clean answer. But I'm increasingly convinced it's a question we need to sit with, because the ambiguity creates tensions that run deeper than scheduling.

**Identity confusion**: Am I someone who loves their work, or someone with poor boundaries? When I genuinely enjoy tinkering with AI tools at 8 PM, am I expressing authentic interest or performing the "passionate employee" that modern tech culture demands? Organizational research on "callings" shows that when people experience work as deeply meaningful, they're more likely to accept exploitation - longer hours, worse conditions, lower pay - because the work "isn't really work" to them. If my AI experimentation feels like a calling, should I be worried?

**Compensation questions**: I'm learning valuable skills on my own time, using tools my employer sometimes provides, which I then apply in my job. Who owns that value? Should I be compensated for the Sunday afternoon I spent mastering prompt engineering? What about the three hours of ERG photography work? Classical labor economics had clear answers: if you're paid by the hour and you work extra hours, you get paid extra. If you're salaried, there's an implicit understanding about reasonable expectations. But what's "reasonable" when the work is intrinsically motivating, personally enriching, and happens to benefit your employer?

**Autonomy vs. exploitation**: Self-directed learning feels empowering. I *choose* to explore these tools. Nobody's forcing me. Except... there's a quiet obligation creeping in. When colleagues mention their weekend AI experiments in team meetings, when job descriptions start listing "demonstrated curiosity about emerging technologies," when promotion criteria include "continuous learning" - suddenly my "choice" to tinker on Tuesday nights feels less autonomous. The psychological contract - those unspoken expectations between employer and employee - has shifted without anyone explicitly renegotiating the terms.

**The quiet obligation**: Research on organizational citizenship behavior shows that companies massively benefit from employees' discretionary efforts - the ERG work, the after-hours learning, the knowledge sharing - while successfully maintaining the fiction that these contributions are "voluntary." It's a brilliant (and troubling) piece of organizational sleight-of-hand: we celebrate and reward these behaviors while refusing to formalize or compensate them, which allows the company to extract value without obligation.

**Organizational free-riding**: When [Gallup analyzed the "quiet quitting" phenomenon](https://www.gallup.com/workplace/349484/state-of-the-global-workplace.aspx) in 2023, they found that 59% of workers globally were "not engaged" or "actively disengaged" - many specifically pushing back against expectations to go above and beyond without recognition. The pendulum has swung from "always-on hustle culture" to "do exactly what you're paid for, nothing more." But that binary feels unsatisfying too. I don't want to quiet-quit activities I genuinely enjoy. I just want clarity about what's expected, what's optional, and what's fairly compensated.

Philosopher Hannah Arendt distinguished between "labor" (activities for survival), "work" (world-building creation), and "action" (meaningful engagement with others). Modern employment conflates all three, which creates ontological confusion about what we're actually doing when we sit at our laptops. Am I laboring to earn a paycheck? Working to build something meaningful? Acting to connect with a professional community? The answer is probably "all of the above," which is precisely why "is this work?" is such a hard question to answer.


## A Better Question

I don't have a tidy solution to this mess. Anyone who promises one is either selling something or hasn't thought it through. But I've been experimenting with a reframe that helps.

Instead of asking "Is this work?", I'm trying to ask: **"Is this aligned with my values right now?"**

That question opens up three more specific diagnostics:

**1. Energy: Does this drain or replenish me?**  
Sometimes coding at 8 PM energizes me - I'm in flow, learning, creating. Other times, I'm forcing myself to "stay current" because I feel behind, and it's pure depletion. Same activity, totally different experience. The timestamp doesn't tell me whether it's healthy. My energy level does.

**2. Choice: Am I doing this because I want to or because I feel I should?**  
There's a world of difference between "I'm curious about this tool" and "Everyone else is experimenting and I'll look outdated if I don't." Research shows that autonomous motivation (I want to) leads to better outcomes and wellbeing than controlled motivation (I should), even when the behavior looks identical from the outside. The question isn't whether someone expects this of me - expectations exist. The question is whether I've genuinely internalized those expectations or whether I'm performing compliance.

**3. Reciprocity: Is the value exchange fair?**  
If I spend Sunday afternoon on ERG work that genuinely feeds my soul and connects me to my community, that's different from spending Sunday afternoon on ERG work because I'm worried it'll hurt my promotion chances if I don't. Both involve unpaid labor. One feels reciprocal (I get meaning, community gets contribution). The other feels extractive (company gets labor, I get anxiety about career consequences). The fairness question doesn't have an objective answer - it requires ongoing negotiation with myself and, ideally, with my employer.

Now when I'm tinkering with Amp at 8 PM, I check in: Am I energized or depleted? Did I choose this or am I avoiding difficult feelings about something else? Would I do this if it had zero career benefit, purely for the joy of learning? 

Sometimes the answers mean keep going - I'm exactly where I want to be. Sometimes they mean close the laptop - I'm performing productivity instead of resting. Both answers are valid.

This isn't about drawing a hard line at 5:30 PM. It's about drawing a conscious line wherever it needs to be for me, on this particular day, given my actual energy and actual choices. The boundary isn't about time or location. It's about agency and sustainability.


## Living in the Liminal

Anthropologist Victor Turner coined the term "liminality" to describe threshold states - the space between what was and what's coming, where old structures don't apply but new ones haven't solidified. Weddings, graduations, career transitions. Times when you're "betwixt and between."

We're living in liminal space when it comes to work. The old definition (manager-assigned, location-bound, time-delimited) has dissolved. The new definition hasn't emerged yet. And trying to force old boundaries onto new realities creates more stress than simply acknowledging the ambiguity.

That doesn't mean giving up on boundaries altogether. Research on burnout consistently shows that psychological detachment - the ability to mentally disconnect from work during off-hours - is critical for recovery and wellbeing. Always-on culture isn't sustainable, even when the "on" time feels enjoyable. The dopamine hits from AI-assisted wins can mask genuine depletion until we crash.

But the goal isn't to eliminate the gray zone. It's to navigate it consciously.

Some organizations are ahead of this curve. Countries like France have legally enshrined workplace boundaries, requiring companies to negotiate policies about after-hours contact. Some organizations have implemented structural solutions - limiting email access outside work hours or establishing clear response-time expectations. These are acknowledgments that individual willpower isn't enough when the technology actively resists boundaries.

Other companies are experimenting with formalizing gray-zone activities. Some organizations now count employee resource group leadership toward performance goals or recognize diversity work in promotion criteria. Others run dedicated innovation days where experimental tinkering is explicitly work time, not nights-and-weekends volunteering. These policies codify what "counts" instead of leaving it ambiguous.

It's 8 PM. I'm on my laptop, writing this blog about work boundaries. The irony isn't lost on me.

But here's what's different from that opening scene: I've checked in with myself. I'm energized, not depleted. I chose this writing project because it genuinely interests me, not because I felt obligated. The value exchange feels reciprocal - I'm clarifying my own thinking, and if it helps someone else navigate their own liminal space, that's a gift.

For tonight, that's enough. And when it's not - when the energy shifts or the choice feels forced or the reciprocity breaks - I'll notice. And I'll close the laptop.

The question isn't "is this work?" The question is "is this aligned with who I want to be and how I want to spend my finite energy?"

Sometimes that means coding until midnight because I'm genuinely lit up. Sometimes it means strict shutdown rituals because I need to protect my rest. Both are valid. Both are necessary. Both require me to keep asking.

We're living in the liminal space. It's uncomfortable. It's ambiguous. It's not going away anytime soon.

But maybe - just maybe - we can navigate it consciously instead of letting the defaults decide for us.
