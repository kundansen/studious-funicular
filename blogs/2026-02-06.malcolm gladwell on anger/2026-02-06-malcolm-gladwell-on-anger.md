# Malcolm Gladwell on Anger

*February 6, 2026*

---

## The Elizabeth Cady Stanton Story: How Uncontrolled Rage Delayed Women's Suffrage by 50 Years

*A summary of Malcolm Gladwell's talk at the Tilles Center, Brookville, Long Island - February 5, 2026*

---

On a cold February evening on Long Island, Malcolm Gladwell delivered a talk centered on a single, often-neglected emotion: anger. Not the righteous indignation that fuels social movements, but the kind of anger that betrays you - that makes you say things you don't mean and do things you'll regret.

Gladwell spent 90 minutes walking through one of the most consequential stories in American history, using the life of Elizabeth Cady Stanton to illustrate how anger, left unchecked, can derail even the most noble causes. He contrasted her story with that of Jackie Robinson, showing what happens when anger is controlled with discipline and purpose.

**The Making of a Revolutionary**

Gladwell began with Elizabeth Cady Stanton's origin story. Born into privilege in upstate New York, she became the son her father no longer had after losing his only male heir. She tried to prove herself as smart as any boy, but her father's disappointment was clear: she could never inherit, never attend college, never be what he wanted-because she was a woman.

"I'm as smart as any boy I know," Stanton told her father. "I'm just not allowed an education. Why? Because I'm a woman."

That fury, Gladwell explained, would never leave her. It would propel her to become one of the most famous women in 19th-century America.

At 24, she married Henry Stanton, a prominent abolitionist. It was an unusually modern marriage-he treated her as an equal. She even called him by his first name in public, which was scandalous for the era. Together, they traveled to London in 1840 for the World Anti-Slavery Convention, where 500 delegates gathered to fight against slavery.

**The Betrayal in London**

What happened next would change American history.

The convention organizers announced that while they had "the highest regard for the ladies," women would not be allowed to participate as official delegates. They would have to sit in a segregated area, silent observers to a cause they'd fought for.

And who went along with that decision? Henry Stanton.

"Look, we're here to fight slavery," the leaders said. "We're not here to fight for the equality of women. That's too much."

Gladwell paused. "That sounds very much like today, doesn't it?"

In that moment, Elizabeth Cady Stanton realized she needed to be part of a much broader conversation about women's rights. Until that was addressed, she would never be treated as an equal. She returned to America and became one of the founders of the women's suffrage movement.

**The Partnership: Stanton, Anthony, and Douglass**

Stanton teamed up with Susan B. Anthony, and for the next 50 years, the two were inseparable. They became the two great pioneers of the fight for women's voting rights, the reason women can vote today. By 1900, if you'd asked someone to name the ten most famous people in America, Elizabeth Cady Stanton would be on that list.

The other person on that list was Frederick Douglass.

Born into slavery in Maryland, Douglass endured unimaginable hardship before escaping North and becoming the most powerful voice against slavery. Douglass saw the fight for women's rights as part of the same struggle for equality. At the 1848 Seneca Falls Convention, where Stanton drafted her Declaration of Sentiments-"We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men and women are created equal"-Douglass was the only Black man present. He stood up and signed it.

"This is right," he said. "Women should be able to vote."

They made common cause. The three of them-Stanton, Anthony, and Douglass-became warriors for justice.

**Why We Don't Talk About Anger**

At this point, Gladwell pivoted to his central theme.

"I think we talk a lot about love and happiness," he said. "We talk a lot about jealousy and temptation. We talk a lot about joy. But I don't think we give proper attention to anger-those moments when we or the people around us do things that we later regret because we've been angry."

Most emotions, he explained, are internal. If you know who someone loves or what brings them joy, you know something crucial about them. Those emotions are authentic, held in the heart.

But anger is different. Anger is outward expression. When you're angry, you're not yourself anymore. The danger of anger is that it betrays you-it makes you say things you don't normally mean. You speak without thinking.

**The 15th Amendment Betrayal**

During the Civil War, Stanton and Anthony threw themselves into the abolitionist cause. With no money and no mass communication, these two women in Rochester managed to gather 400,000 signatures for a constitutional amendment. It was astonishing.

They believed that if constitutional amendments could grant equality between Black and white Americans, it was a short step to equality between men and women. The two causes were connected. Everything was aligned.

Then came the betrayal.

The 15th Amendment was passed, stating that the right to vote "shall not be denied on the basis of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." It was about race. It wasn't about gender. It wasn't about women.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was furious.

"Wait a second," Gladwell recounted her reaction. "I just got you 400,000 signatures. I fought this beautiful anti-slavery battle my whole life because I understood that when we freed Black people, women were going to win at the same time. These issues were joined together. And now what are we doing? We're cutting it in half."

She said she'd rather cut off her right hand than support the amendment as written.

**The Turn to Racism**

What happened next is where Gladwell's story became uncomfortable.

Stanton sought help from anyone who would support her cause. She turned to George Francis Train, one of the most vicious racists in American history. Many people in 19th-century America were racist, Gladwell acknowledged. Mark Twain was a genuine racist. But George Francis Train was THE racist-unbelievably loathsome on every level.

Train's mission statement: "Why should a bunch of ignorant Black men from the South get to vote before you?"

And Stanton agreed.

"Yes! You're absolutely right," she said. "You're a brilliant, educated woman from the very best of families. And some guy two generations out of slavery in the South-some illiterate former slave-is going to get the vote before me? I think that's wrong. There's no justice in that."

She and Train went on tour together. 200 gatherings. Thousands of people came. She allied herself with someone who represented everything they'd been fighting against-someone actively working to suppress Black votes, to abolish the 15th Amendment entirely.

She said things like: "This white woman is educated. This Black man is ignorant. We are a people of blood, of education, of refinement and civilization. But if it comes down to letting ignorant Black people from the South vote, I'm going to stand up and fight it. I think it's abominable."

Gladwell read from one of her speeches:

"When women-because they are women-are hunted down through the cities of New York and New Orleans, when they are dragged from their houses and hung upon lamp posts, when their children are torn from their arms and their brains dashed out upon the pavement, when they are objects of insult everywhere they go and are in danger-they'll have their right to protection from government. But until then..."

Gladwell stopped. "In 1865, people are being hung by racist mobs in the South from trees right now. And she is more concerned about her own disenfranchisement."

Frederick Douglass was absolutely right, Gladwell said. The cause of racial equality had to be prioritized. But Stanton couldn't see it. Her anger had poisoned her.

**The Cost: 50 Years**

"Do you know when women got the right to vote?" Gladwell asked. "1920. Fifty years later. Half a century."

That incredible movement suffered a massive setback. It wasn't just bad luck or timing. It was a consequence of where anger took Elizabeth Cady Stanton and how that anger derailed the cause.

The rhetoric was cheap. The vitriol set back the cause of freedom. And the suffrage movement paid the price.

**The Counterpoint: Jackie Robinson**

Gladwell then shifted to a different story-a story about controlling anger.

Jackie Robinson grew up fighting injustice. He got into altercations. He defended Black soldiers facing discrimination in the army and faced court-martial. All his life, he'd stood up for himself. He had pride.

Then Branch Rickey, general manager of the Brooklyn Dodgers, called him in 1945. Rickey wanted to break the color line in baseball, and he wanted Robinson to be the one to do it.

"Be aware that if we do this, we're going to face hell," Rickey told him.

Robinson remembered the conversation vividly. "Could I take all this abuse and control my temper? Remain silent and not fight back? He knew I was going to face it every day-a mind game to see if I could handle hatred."

"All my life, back to my childhood, I had always stood up for myself. The real challenge was-could I go on like this for years? Hold it all inside? Pride is the richest treasure any man has."

"Do you want a ball player who's afraid?" Robinson asked.

Rickey's response: "I want a ball player with guts enough not to fight back."

"You've got to not give in to your anger. If they throw at your head-don't charge the mound. Don't retaliate. Jackie, you remember one thing: no matter what happens on the ball field, you can't fight back. That's when you've lost."

The conviction of bringing Robinson onto the team, of making him the face of breaking the color barrier, depended on him never being angry. No matter how bad they were to him. No matter how much they provoked him. No matter how much they hated or insulted him. He could not-ever-respond.

**The Ordeal**

Everything Rickey predicted came true.

The things thrown at Jackie Robinson those first two years were unbelievable. People threw objects at him. They shouted slurs. They aimed to injure him when he slid. He couldn't take revenge. He couldn't strike back.

Crowds yelled obscenities at him from the front row, calling him animals, throwing watermelon and fried chicken. When the team traveled, he couldn't stay in the same hotels because of segregation. People tried to injure him on the field.

"I did it because I loved the cause," Robinson said later. "Because I knew what was at stake. And Rickey was right. I couldn't do it if I was going to respond."

If he'd given in to his anger-if he'd fought back when provoked, if he'd gotten into fights, if he'd gone to jail-the movement would have failed.

"You've violated the one rule you can never violate when you are involved in a struggle like this," Gladwell explained. "It's a form of self-destruction. You can't let hate-you cannot let the oppressor say you have to hate them back. You can be angry. There's nothing wrong with feeling anger. But if you wanted the movement to succeed, is anger the right part of the equation?"

**The Final Betrayal: Douglass's Marriage**

There's one more chapter to Stanton's story.

In 1884, Frederick Douglass-now one of the most famous men in America, an elder statesman of the fight for equality-married a young white woman. Interracial marriage was illegal in most places. It was considered immoral, taboo. The country was decades away from accepting it.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was furious.

"Why would you marry a white woman?" she demanded. "This is wrong."

She wrote him a letter. She wrote to her friends. She said she'd publish it publicly and tell the whole world they wouldn't stand by and accept the scandal.

"What does having a family have to do with anything?" Gladwell asked. "What does it matter to anyone what this man does in his private life? She couldn't have any perspective-because of what they might say or what judgment might be passed."

It was anger speaking. And for her, it was a scarring moment. She was enraged. She couldn't see anything beyond it.

Years later, she looked back with regret.

"I can't remember all of them. I don't know what to do with that regret. I never made amends for that. I don't know what to do with that burden, with that legacy. It's almost a lifetime ago. I didn't think-how could I have let anger turn me into that? How could I have said those things?"

**The Lesson**

Gladwell's message was clear: Anger is not inherently wrong. You can feel it. You should feel it when confronting injustice.

But anger that controls you-anger that makes you say things you don't mean, that allies you with people who represent everything you've fought against, that blinds you to the larger struggle-that kind of anger destroys movements and delays progress by generations.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton was a brilliant, courageous pioneer who changed American history. But her inability to control her anger in critical moments cost the women's suffrage movement 50 years. It cost her friendship with Frederick Douglass. It left her with regrets she carried to her grave.

Jackie Robinson faced unimaginable hatred and had every right to be angry. But he chose discipline over rage. He chose the cause over his pride. And he succeeded in changing American sports and American society because of it.

The difference between the two stories is the difference between anger that derails and discipline that transforms.

As Gladwell put it: "You can't let the oppressor make you hate them back. You can be angry. But don't let it destroy what you're fighting for."

**Malcolm Gladwell** is the author of seven New York Times bestsellers, including *The Tipping Point*, *Blink*, *Outliers*, and *Talking to Strangers*. He is also the host of the podcast *Revisionist History* and co-founder of Pushkin Industries. This talk was part of his speaking tour exploring the hidden dynamics of human behavior and historical turning points.
