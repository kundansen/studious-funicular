# Edge, Boredom, and the Unreasonable

*October 28, 2025*

## Four Lines I Underlined (and Why They Still Bother Me)

I've been revisiting the little notes I jot down in my Notes app when I read or listen to my books—the quotes, the hopeful stars, and the exasperated "???" that I reserve for ideas I might not agree with but probably should. A few sentences from recent reading sessions (especially from *Atomic Habits*) keep floating over my Notes like polite little gnats. They're neat, quotable, and honestly, a bit inconvenient because they happen to be true.

Here are four of them and what they seem to be doing to my work and life: not reforming me exactly, just nudging the room a few inches so the furniture fits better.

---

### The Goldilocks rule: We experience peak motivation when working on tasks right on the edge of our current abilities

This sentence has a subtle way of stirring the pot. It gently raises my awareness of my own extremes. When I feel numb, it might mean I'm not being challenged enough, much like those TPS reports from Office Space. When I'm crafting grand speeches in my mind about how nobody gets my brilliance, it's probably a sign I've gone too far. Finding that middle ground—somewhere between yawning and panicking—isn't usually announced with fanfare. Instead, it's more like tuning a radio that's almost on the station but still a bit fuzzy at the edges.

Recently, I've been trying out small adjustments—nothing drastic, just measurable tweaks. For example, I might cut a paragraph by ten percent without losing its meaning, or shave a few seconds off a workflow I use daily. I also explain the same concept to someone five years earlier in their career and notice exactly where I start to waffle. When I focus on such tiny changes, two wonderful things happen: I can recognize a small victory before lunch, and my internal narration becomes peaceful. There's no room for that voice saying, 'What we need here is a revolution," which tends to lead to frustration. Even my virtual gym coach would agree with these simple improvements.

The "just-beyond" zone turns out to be oddly energizing. Not thrilling, not terrifying—just oxygenating. You get to feel mostly competent while being slightly in over your head. It's not a romance novel, but it's a livable marriage with your attention. The trap is that the edge can start to feel ordinary, and ordinary can look suspiciously like boredom, which brings me to the quote that made me put the book down and stare at the wall.

---

### The greatest threat to success is not failure but boredom

Failure is cinematic like a classic Bollywood drama. You can gather around it. Boredom is what the camera cuts away from. Yet, if anything good has stayed in my life, it has learned to survive on thin rations of excitement. The weekly write-up nobody asks for but everyone quietly reads. The dull note I leave for next month's me so I don't repeat a mistake with great conviction. The unglamorous fix to the thing that always breaks at 3:58 p.m.

This line honestly doesn't make habits sound overly glamorous. It gently reminds us that having a nice notebook doesn't turn routine into a burst of dopamine. Instead, it's about understanding that if you keep doing something helpful consistently, it'll become as natural as brushing your teeth while thinking about dinner. That feeling isn't failure — it's your system doing its job smoothly, without needing any applause.

I'm trying to stop treating boredom like an enemy to duel and started treating it like the weather. There are breezy days. There are gray ones. The actual trick—at least for me—is letting a little air into the room. A change of soundtrack. A change of time slot. A slightly ridiculous micro-reward at the end, like a five-minute video of someone restoring a vintage toaster to gleaming righteousness. I'm not proud, but look at that pivot!

Motivation, as it beautifully bursts onto the scene, can sometimes be a bit less reliable when it comes to everyday chores. If boredom is the real challenge, then what you need is not a spectacular fireworks display, but a cozy chair—comfortable spots and small routines that make redoing things feel natural rather than extraordinary. But that doesn't mean we should settle for every default setting. Occasionally, the furniture isn't quite right—perhaps the entire layout needs a rethink. And at those moments, an old Irish playwright might step in with a straightforward line and a knowing raise of the eyebrow, offering a fresh perspective.

---

### George Bernard Shaw: "The reasonable man adapts himself to the world; the unreasonable one persists in trying to adapt the world to himself. Therefore, all progress depends on the unreasonable man."

I love this line the way you love a rabbit that occasionally eats your HDMI or Ethernet cables (ours do both, repeatedly). It can be noble or it can be insufferable, and the difference is invisible from across the street. There's the kind of "unreasonable" that shouts, and then there's the kind that quietly refuses to make peace with a foolish default. Only one of them tends to leave the campsite cleaner.

As I grow older, I realize that progress often comes from small, thoughtful refusals. Most of the quirkiest parts of a system aren't the result of deliberate plans; they happen because someone's busy, tired, or simply copying what others do. If you try to accept everything, you might end up gathering a collection of outdated exceptions. On the other hand, if you try to challenge everything, nothing gets finished. For me, the key is to pick one principle to stand by—kindly and consistently—and see the rest as part of the context, not as battles to win.

I kept things simple for a long time: I'd write down the decision before diving into the implementation. It's not about making you a better person, but about helping your future self be less confused. It's a small act that might take a moment but can make everything else flow smoothly. Shaw's message, when taken with kindness, isn't that you should be difficult; it's that you should choose to be difficult consciously. Focus on one challenge at a time. Enjoy some popcorn. Speak gently.

If you insist on better patterns, you have to accept the last, uncomfortable piece: new things don't just arrive; they push old things off the table. Not scoot them. Push.

---

### Originality is an act of creative destruction

In headlines, "creative destruction" often seems charming, like a turtleneck and a keynote speech. But when you take a closer look, it's more about clearing out the old, sometimes painfully, like a death certificate. The exciting part—the creation of something new—gets all the attention. Meanwhile, the important work—letting go of the old carefully and permanently—is where the true maturity and care reside.

I used to think originality was about piling on more ideas, features, and artifacts. But lately, it feels more like creating space — which can be a bit painful. That space often has sentimental value; someone always cherishes the old hallway because their desk is there, or a weekend project lives in that corner. There are photos that tell stories. But remember, complexity has a cost, and unpaid dues eventually come due, testing patience and understanding. Having three ways to do the same thing might seem inclusive, but it can also create the illusion of speed, while behind the scenes, the plumbing quietly groans and clanks.

The people I admire most are uniquely skilled at saying goodbye with grace. They retire things thoughtfully, taking the time to explain why. They leave gentle hints, like breadcrumbs, so if someone future looked back, they could understand the story behind the ruins. Then, they embrace the tranquil new chapter. That quiet isn't emptiness; it's like a runway, ready for new beginnings. Whenever I see a plan only growing, I wonder if someone is avoiding dealing with grief. The plans I really trust are the ones that thoughtfully add something new and let go of something old—both with sincerity and clarity.

### How These Lines Ganged Up on Me

I didn't want them to connect. I'm unsure if they were all from Atomic Habits (I missed noting the source). I preferred each quote as a tasteful coaster I could set my coffee on. But they keep forming a small conspiracy.

The Goldilocks edge shifts how we experience a day. I stop confusing adrenaline with progress and focus on the moment where attention can truly expand. In this view, boredom is simply the cost of repetition. Paying it yields compound benefits; ignoring it results in a new hobby every month. Shaw's stubborn builder appears to repair the doorframe that keeps sticking—delivering a firm "no" to facilitate many easier "yeses." Creative destruction involves removing unusable walls so the door can swing open fully.

Together, they create a space that avoids turning into drama. Remove the sharpness, and I lean toward theatrical ambitions and cinematic gloom. Reject boredom, and I seek out sparks, confusing them with a campfire. Mislabel "unreasonable," and I become someone others avoid in the hallway. Avoid destruction, and I stumble over the museum exhibit I keep saying I will move later.

I notice a change in my calendar. It hasn't become minimalist but still resembles a Tetris game played with optimistic chaos (I'd share a screenshot if confidentiality allowed). Inside the blocks, there's less unnecessary clutter. I no longer wait for a perfect moment. Instead, I choose one task and make a quick adjustment. I avoid debating whether routines warrant special treatment. I complete them, ideally before my morning plan expires. I don't start each day with a fight against the status quo; I maintain one firm refusal and let it quietly influence the environment. Each week, I aim—still trying—to remove one old artifact, so the new can settle without having to navigate past its predecessors.

If that sounds a bit like discipline, I understand where you're coming from. Discipline often gets a bad rap, always dressed in gray. But within these simple four lines, it feels less like a stern duty and more like a kind, everyday rule—a little bit of order that makes the space cozy. Shoes off at the door, lights on when you leave. Nothing grand or inspirational—just little habits that keep the floor tidy enough for a spontaneous dance if the music happens to start playing.

None of this is a conversion narrative. I still get the urge to flip a table and reinvent my schedule on a Wednesday afternoon because a stranger on the internet bought a new pen. I still believe in sweeping gestures. But the quotes have taught me to ask a smaller question first: if I move this one dial, will tomorrow be lighter? Sometimes the answer is yes. Sometimes the answer is "throw the table out." Both feel more honest than the old habit of pacing while delivering a TED Talk to an imaginary audience.

---

### Afterword: A Line in Pencil

If I had to add one sentence of my own to the margin, it would be this: ***Most of adulthood is arranging your surroundings so your better intentions have shorter commutes than your worse ones***.

That's what the edge does—it shortens the commute for attention. Boredom, treated as weather instead of a scandal, shortens the commute for consistency. Shaw's "unreasonable" stance shortens the commute for clarity by fixing one door everyone keeps bumping into. And creative destruction demolishes the tollbooths we forgot we were paying at—old interfaces, old rituals, old debris that slows the day without ever filing an expense report.

I don't expect a medal for any of it. The best part, oddly, is the lack of spectacle. Progress stops auditioning. It starts sounding like a room where the chair is in the right place, the window opens, and the door swings without complaint. The work you meant to do shows up, removes its shoes, and gets on with it. No montage, no theme music. Just the quiet pleasure of a day that fits.
