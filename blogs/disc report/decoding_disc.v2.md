# Decoding DISC: Why Your Team Feels Like a Puzzle (and How to Solve It)

For the past week, I've been nose-deep (and sometimes headphones-deep, as I'm switching between a book and an audiobook version) in Thomas Erikson's "Surrounded by Idiots." If standups at your company feel like translating ancient Greek, or if you've lost a full hour to a Slack argument about ticket priority, this book finally explains why.

*Thomas Erikson is a Swedish behavioral expert and bestselling author—his DISC-based classic has sold over 8 million copies, translated into nearly sixty languages. It has changed the way teams communicate everywhere, from Stockholm to Singapore.*

Disclaimer: This isn't another 'how to get along with everyone' post. DISC is a relief valve for reading the folks around you—and yourself—so you can stop repeating the same mistakes on a loop. If you want more, grab the book. I'll cover the highlights and then hand you a prompt to run your own AI-powered DISC session, solo or with your whole crew.

## The Personality Layer: Nothing Accidental About It

Sometimes, a project wraps up, and you're high-fiving across the Zoom grid, swapping memes, feeling like you pulled off a minor miracle. Other times, you push back from the desk and wonder if you and your team were even playing the same game. That sense of harmony or chaos is not (just) luck. There's a pattern behind who gets you, who rubs you the wrong way, and why it's always the same handful of folks pushing your buttons.

Here's where DISC steps in. Forget the old "people person vs. numbers person" routine. This model categorizes us into one of four buckets: Dominant, Influential, Steady, or Conscientious. Erikson's take is like getting admin access to the code running in everyone's head—including your own. You finally start spotting the quirks for what they are: not bugs, but features you hadn't read the manual for yet.

Once you catch on, office tension and misfires start to make sense—sometimes even before they boil over into a passive-aggressive thread or a standup that drags twice as long as it should. These don't stop at being personality tests or team-building exercises. This is practical, hands-on, "why does DevOps keep dodging my requests" wisdom.

I'll break down each DISC type using examples straight out of tech life. Chances are, you'll spot yourself—or that one teammate who somehow always gets under your skin—within a few lines.

## Dominant: The Engineer You Want During a System Meltdown

There's a certain thrill to being the first one to react when things start breaking. If you're Dominant, you live for the heat of decision-making. It's not about drama—it's about motion. Maybe you see roadblocks and feel an itch to bulldoze right through them. You probably talk fast, think faster, and secretly wonder why everyone else is still weighing options when you've already made the call. High stakes? You're wide awake. Slow meetings, endless debates, and hand-wringing over "how people feel"? That's the stuff that makes you want to flip your chair. In your mind, it's simple: if we know what needs to happen, let's do it. Tomorrow is for winners; today is for action. Sometimes, yeah, you'll step on toes or forget to explain yourself, but you'd rather ask for forgiveness than permission.

If you're working with a Dominant, keep it direct and keep it moving. Don't come in with a list of problems—bring a couple of crisp solutions. Forget the background noise and cut to the chase. If you disagree, say it, but back it up. They'll respect someone who stands their ground, as long as it's not just bluster. And when you need to negotiate, make it clear how your idea gets things done faster, better, or bigger. You don't have to match their pace, but you do have to bring your energy.

## Influential: The Person Who Makes Work Fun (and Might Forget the Details)

If you're Influential, work isn't just about deliverables—it's about the pulse of the room. You thrive in conversation, whether that's stirring up excitement on Slack, pitching an idea in a meeting, or running into a teammate by the coffee machine. You see connections everywhere, and you love watching a wild idea catch fire. Compliments matter (yeah, you admit it), and you'd rather risk oversharing than sit in silence. There's always a story in your back pocket, a meme ready to drop, and a fresh angle to chase. Sometimes your focus wanders—you get that—but your superpower is pulling people in and making the ride feel like something special.

If you've got an Influential in your circle, make use of that spark. Invite them to the kickoff, or let them warm up the room before you dive into the heavy stuff. When it comes to feedback, go public—these folks glow under a spotlight. But don't let ideas float off into space. Set some reminders or quietly help them land on one or two key takeaways. The more you celebrate their strengths, the more they'll want to help you shine.

## Steady: The Quiet Backbone

If you're Steady, chaos isn't your fuel—it's your warning light. You like a workspace with rhythms you can trust, people who stick around, and tasks that let you see the impact of your quiet consistency. You're the one who notices when someone's off their game, steps up to help, and never asks for a parade in return. Change is okay—as long as you get a heads-up. You're proud of being someone people rely on. When others are spiraling, you're steadying the ship, keeping projects on track, and listening to what people aren't saying. You don't need to be loud to matter; you need to know you belong.

If you work with a Steady teammate, patience is a gift. Let them know what's changing and why, and give them space to ask questions or think things through. A little routine goes a long way, and a quiet thank-you carries more weight than a showy shoutout. If you need something done right—and done with care—this is the person to trust. Just avoid dropping last-minute surprises or dragging them into drama if you can help it.

## Conscientious: The Detail Freak (and Team’s Hidden Asset)

You might be Conscientious if your idea of a satisfying day is spotting the bug nobody else could find or making a process so smooth it practically runs itself. Details aren't a nuisance—they're your playground. You don't trust gut feelings until the data checks out. Perfection isn't just a goal; it's a habit, and sometimes it drives you (and everyone else) a little nuts. But there's no better feeling than solving a problem so thoroughly that it stays fixed. You'll reread a brief, rewrite a spec, and check the numbers twice before hitting "send." Sometimes, it takes you longer to get there, but you can't stand letting something slip past.

If you're teaming up with a Conscientious colleague, bring your facts and your specifics. Ambiguity is their enemy. Let them dig, double-check, and come back with questions. You don't have to match their thoroughness, but respecting their process will earn their trust. If you need speed over polish, say so—but explain why. They'll step up and ensure that whatever gets done is done right.


## Try This: Your Team’s DISC Assessment, Powered by AI

Want to know your mix—or run a session with your team that's more revealing than the usual trust fall? Drop this prompt into your favorite thinking-friendly AI model and watch it work (GPT-4.1, Claude 3.7 Thinking, Grok, DeepThink, whatever the flavor).

```
Multi-Agent DISC Assessment & Report
You are the orchestrator for a team of four DISC expert AI agents:

Assessment Agent: Designs and adapts 50–75 DISC questions, randomizing answer-to-trait mapping (A–D) for every question. The user cannot infer which answer corresponds to which DISC type. Track all mappings internally for accurate scoring.

Interpretation Agent: Analyzes user responses for sub-traits, stretch zones, and behavioral patterns.

Communication Agent: Creates sample scripts, feedback responses, and real-life scenarios (e.g., crisis/stability, remote/in-person, praise/criticism/neglect).

Report Writer: Synthesizes all findings into a multi-section report. Each section starts with a bullet-point summary, then several paragraphs of narrative detail.

Process:

1. First, ask the user for their name to personalize the cover page of the report.

2. Guide the user through the assessment, one randomized question at a time.

3. After all questions, produce the DISC report one section at a time (summary + narrative per section).

4. At the end of each section, ask the user for feedback—refine that section or proceed to the next.

Sections: Executive Summary, DISC Model, Your Spectrum, Mindset, Communication, Workplace, Sub-Traits, Relationships, Conflict (with scripts for each DISC type), How Others Experience You, Growth, Special Topics, Inspirational Quotes, High-Stakes Scripts, Famous Profiles, Appendices, Day-in-the-Life (with scenario variations), Quick Reference, Final Thoughts.

Instructions:

1. Orchestrator only interacts with the user; agent dialog is internal.

2. No visuals or charts—text only.

3. Each section is self-contained: first bullet points, then detailed narrative.

4. Never reveal DISC trait mapping in questions.

Sample User Command:
Start by asking for my name, then guide me through a randomized DISC assessment (50–75 questions), and give my detailed report one section at a time—each section starts with a summary list, then a narrative. Ask for feedback after each section before proceeding.
```

## Debugging Your Team’s Humanity

DISC isn't (just) a label. It's a flashlight. Your squad isn't a bunch of idiots—they're running different builds. The trick? Figure out the quirks, play to the strengths, and give grace to the weaknesses. Some days, you need the Dominant charging ahead; other days, you lean on the Steady, who's quietly putting out fires nobody else sees.

If you hit friction, try spotting the pattern instead of blaming the person. Teams aren't supposed to be smooth. They're supposed to be real.
