# The First 10 Times Are the Toughest: How to Start Something New and Stick With It

*April 6, 2025*

---

I won't forget the first time I tried writing a blog post. I'd been noodling ideas for months—scribbling notes on my desk, jotting down random thoughts on my phone, and dealing with a nagging feeling that I had something worth saying. But when I finally sat down to type, it felt like my brain just… stopped. The words felt stiff, the sentences didn't flow, and I kept doubting every line. It was exhausting, like wading through mud. Still, when I hit "publish," I felt a tiny rush of pride. It was far from perfect, but I'd done it. That messy first step was mine.

Starting anything new—writing, cooking, coding, learning, or whatever—feels like that. The first go is always the roughest. You're stepping into something unfamiliar, and nothing quite fits. But here's what I've learned: it's not just the first try that's hard. The next handful of attempts brings its own struggles—overconfidence, harsh feedback, or the slog when the fun wears off. I'd say the first ten times you tackle something new are the real gauntlet. Push past those, and you're not just hanging in there—you're setting yourself up to improve.

This isn't just me rambling. I've seen it in my own stumbles and in stories from friends, musicians, and even big names who've made it. Those early rounds test you—your patience, your ability to adapt, and your willingness to keep going when it's not exciting anymore. So, let's discuss why those first 10 tries are such a challenge and how to make it through, drawing on a bit of science, some psychology, and a lot of honest grit.

---

### That First Jump: Why It's So Nerve-Wracking

I can still feel the jitters from before I published that first Blogger dot com article in the early 2000s. My cursor hovered over the "publish" button for what felt like forever. *What if it's awful? What if no one cares? What if they hate it?* It's not just about writing or whatever you're trying—it's the fear of screwing up in front of everyone. Psychologists have a name for it: the "beginner's barrier." It's that mental hurdle you've got to drag yourself over just to start.

Consider writing (a blog, an article). You have this idea that you're obsessed with, and you believe it will flow out smoothly because you've daydreamed about it so much. Then you try, and it's a mess. The thoughts don't align, the tone is off, and every typo stands out glaringly. I read somewhere that beginners often think they're better than they are *and* dislike their early work more than they should. It's a tough combination—big dreams colliding with clumsy reality.

But that first shot, flaws and all, is a victory. You did it. You started. I think about someone like Isaac Newton—his early experiments weren't masterpieces. They were messy, full of guesses and fixes. Each one built toward something bigger. Your first try is the same. It's not about nailing it; it's about getting the ball rolling.

---

### The Next Couple: Tripping After the Start

You make it through the first try—awesome! But don't relax yet. The second and third go-rounds can trick you. After my first post, I got a little full of myself. "I've got this," I thought. The next one should've been a breeze. Nope. I rushed it, and it was a disaster—disconnected ideas, sloppy phrasing. I'd gotten cocky too soon.

Then there's feedback. If you share your work, the comments can sting. My third post got a note about a dumb mistake I'd missed. I felt like an idiot. It's tempting to take it personally, to think you're not cut out for it. But that's when you've got to toughen up. Criticism isn't a dead end; it's a nudge to get better. Even The Beatles had rough early gigs—they didn't quit after a bad night.

---

### The Middle Bit: When It Stops Being Fun

By the fourth or fifth try, the shine wears off. For me, after a few posts, my easy ideas were spent. I'd sit there, staring at a blank screen, wondering what to say next. It's like when you're learning guitar and your fingers ache, or your tomato plants start dying, and you don't know why. The thrill's gone, and it's just work.

This is where it gets real. The newness fades, and you're left with the grind. I've heard about this thing called the "plateau"—when your progress stalls, and it feels pointless. It's brutal, and a lot of people bail here. But if you stick it out, this is where you actually grow. It's not glamorous—it's showing up when you'd rather not. I think of someone like Elon Musk; Tesla didn't happen because he chased the high. It came from slogging through one rough draft after another.

---

### Why 10? It's a Thing

Why 10 times, though? It's not some sacred rule, but I've noticed a pattern. In music, you've got one-hit wonders who fade fast, then legends who keep going. There's not much in between—either they quit early, or they hit double digits. Same with inventors. I saw a study in *Nature* once that said the best ones often hit their stride after tons of tries. The first 10 are like boot camp—you mess up, learn, and figure out what sticks. Get through that, and you're on another level.

---

### How to Keep Going: Stuff That's Worked for Me

So, how do you survive those first 10 without giving up? Here's what's helped me:

- **Pat yourself on the back.** After each try, I note one good thing. Even if it's just "I finished," that counts.
- **Keep it small.** Big goals overwhelm me, so I focus on tiny ones. For writing, it's "get 200 words down." Little wins pile up.
- **Brace for the slump.** I know the fifth or sixth try will suck. I expect it now—maybe grab a coffee or step away. It's normal, not failure.
- **Find a crew.** Talking to others who get it helps. I joined a writing group on Facebook, and hearing their struggles kept me sane.
- **Celebrate 10.** When I hit my 10th post, I treated myself to a new bottle of Scotch. It wasn't flawless, but I'd made it. Mark that milestone however you want.
- **Discipline, not motivation.** Motivation comes and goes. Discipline sticks.

---

### What You Get Out of It

Here's the payoff: after those 10 tries, it changes. The nerves ease up, the work feels less impossible, and you see progress. By my 11th post, writing wasn't a battle anymore. It wasn't effortless, but it was doable. I'd built a rhythm and some confidence.

It's more than just getting better at the thing. You come out tougher, more flexible, a bit bolder. That's worth every stumble. So, if you're hesitating to start something new, know this: the first 10 times will push you. They'll make you question yourself, trip you up, maybe even make you laugh at how bad it gets. But they'll also turn you into someone who can handle the next 10. And by the 11th? You might just be the one cheering someone else on.
