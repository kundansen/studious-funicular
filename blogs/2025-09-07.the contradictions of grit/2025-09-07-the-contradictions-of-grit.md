# The Contradictions of Grit: Why Success Belongs to Those Who Stay

*September 7, 2025*

### The Myth of Easy Success

We're suckers for the overnight success story. We love hearing about the kid who gets recruited by Google before finishing college, or the musician who posts one song on TikTok and wakes up famous. Talent and speed make great headlines, and feed our dopamine-craving noggins.

Once you look beyond the headlines, you'll see that the full story is often more nuanced. **Angela Duckworth's Grit** is essentially a heartfelt tribute to the value of slow and steady progress — the kind that may seem dull at first glance but becomes truly inspiring when you look back. Her research highlights that it's not always the brightest or most talented individuals who go the farthest, but those who hold on and never give up.

That sounds almost un-American, doesn't it? Stick with one thing? For decades? When immersed in pivots, "shift-left", and "fail fast"? Yet that's what the evidence says. It's also why I see *Grit* fitting neatly alongside books I've read or listened to recently, and written about on my blogs: ***Blink*** on intuition, ***Atomic Habits*** on daily behaviors, and ***Originals*** on daring ideas. They're all different cuts of the same diamond: the hidden levers of success are rarely what we think.

---

### Talent vs. Effort: The Great Reversal

Wondered what happened to that kid in your class who was a genius at math but somehow didn't "make it"? Duckworth would say they ran out of effort.

Her achievement equation is deceptively simple:

> Talent × Effort = Skill

> Skill × Effort = Achievement

**Effort counts twice.** Talent can get you noticed, but effort turns potential into something real.

The contradiction here is almost cruel: being naturally talented can actually backfire. The "gifted" often coast early, while the grinders build stamina. Later, when challenges get harder, the grinders pull ahead.

Think about the spelling bee champions Duckworth studied. They weren't born with photographic memories. They just put in more hours with word lists. It's not glamorous — unless memorizing "appoggiatura" is your thing — but it worked.

This is where her idea overlaps with *Atomic Habits*. James Clear showed how small daily acts compound each other. Duckworth explains *why* they matter: because effort is the real multiplier. Talent is like seed money; effort is compound interest.

---

### Passion vs. Discipline: The Slow Burn

Tiny confession: I used to roll my eyes at the "find your passion" advice. It always felt like waiting for lightning to strike. Duckworth agrees. Most people don't "find" passion fully formed. They trip over a spark and then build it, slowly, through years of tinkering.

But sparks die out fast if you don't tend them. Passion without discipline is a campfire with no wood. Gritty people, Duckworth shows, stick with their passion through the boring parts — the scales, the code rewrites, the PoC refinements to align to a business case, the endless drills.

Discipline isn't stubbornness, though. It's a consistent direction with flexible tactics. The gritty aren't afraid to quit *side* pursuits if they distract from the main goal. Quitting, in that sense, is a strategy.

Here I'm reminded of *Originals*. Adam Grant showed that originality doesn't come from chasing every idea — it comes from refining one over and over. Duckworth reframes that as discipline: the unglamorous daily return to the thing that matters most.

This is where grit looks boring. But boring is underrated. Excellence is repetitive — like brushing your teeth. You don't cheer when you floss, but your dentist sure notices.

---

### Purpose vs. Pleasure: What Really Sustains Grit

Pleasure is great for getting started. Purpose is what keeps you in the game. Duckworth found that the people who stuck longest with their work tied it to something bigger than themselves — teaching kids, curing diseases, protecting the planet.

This is the part of *Grit* that made me pause and replay the chapter. We live in a culture that worships fun. Work should be "engaging" and "exciting." But Duckworth argues that grit doesn't come from chasing fun — it comes from finding meaning. She says, "Finding passion at work is a little bit of discovery, a lot of development, and a lifetime of deepening."

Obsession isn't always unhealthy. The gritty often have a single-minded obsession with one thing. They're not well-rounded; they're deeply invested. And that's exactly why they break through.

Identity is key here. James, in *Atomic Habits*, argued that lasting change happens when behaviors become identity: "I'm the kind of person who…" Duckworth's gritty achievers live this. They don't just *do* their craft; they *are* their craft.

Coco Gauff is the perfect modern example. Winning the US Open at 19 wasn't about sudden talent. It was years of repetition, wrapped in the identity of being a tennis player. Pleasure faded long ago; purpose carried her through.

### Failure vs. Hope: The Feedback Loop of Grit

The paradox: gritty people fail more, not less. They simply use failure differently.

Duckworth calls failure "information." The gritty don't see setbacks as a verdict on their ability. They see them as a feedback loop. Each stumble is raw data — messy, painful, but useful.

Grit grows with age. Unlike IQ, which stays stable, grit can increase as you get older and reframe failure. Hope, in this sense, isn't optimism. It's the conviction that setbacks are temporary.

Think of the teachers Duckworth studied. Many left the profession after only a few years. But the ones who stayed — who turned bad days into feedback rather than proof they weren't cut out for it — became exceptional educators.

Take startups. Some of the most enduring companies (Slack, for example) started as failed products. Their founders didn't interpret failure as "we're doomed." They reframed, adapted, and tried again. That's grit in action.

Gladwell's *Blink* showed how intuition sharpens with practice. Duckworth argues grit works the same way — it's not something you're born with, it's something you strengthen by surviving cycles of failure.

---

### Consistency vs. Intensity: The Long Game

If you want fireworks, watch a sprint. If you want impact, watch a marathon. Duckworth found that the most meaningful success is rarely about intensity — it's about consistency.

That idea grates against hustle culture. We glorify intensity: the all-nighter, the viral launch, the 90-day transformation. But Duckworth shows that daily effort, repeated for years, beats short-term sprints every time.

The paradox is that structure actually frees creativity. Writers who hit the desk at 8 a.m. daily end up producing the most original work. Musicians who drill scales are the ones who can improvise brilliantly. Consistency lays the tracks for creativity to run wild.

Take Patagonia. For fifty years, they've stuck to environmental responsibility. While competitors chased trends, Patagonia's consistency built trust — and trust became loyalty.

Adam Grant argued in *Originals* that originality isn't lightning, it's persistence. Duckworth closes the loop: grit is what lets persistence stretch across decades.

Honestly, this is the hardest contradiction of all. Consistency is boring. But so is flossing. And both work.

---

### The Contradiction That Works

At West Point, cadets endure "Beast Barracks," seven brutal weeks meant to break them down. Duckworth studied those who survived. It wasn't the tallest, fastest, or smartest. It was the ones who simply refused to quit.

That's the contradiction of grit: it feels dull up close, but dazzling in the long run.

Across *Atomic Habits*, *Blink*, *Originals*, and now *Grit*, the pattern is clear. Success hides in counterintuitive places. Not in hacks. Not in brilliance. But in the willingness to keep showing up long after excitement has faded.

So maybe the most radical thing you can do in a world of pivots is… not pivot. Stay with the practice. Stay with the purpose. Stay long enough to see what consistency can turn you into.

Because grit isn't grinding yourself down. It's building a life that compounds — slowly, invisibly — until it suddenly looks like success happened overnight.
