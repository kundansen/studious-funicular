## The Blog Post

I’ve spent the last two weeks reading (and listening to) Cal Newport’s *Deep Work*. The book argues that true value in our economy comes from "Deep Work"-professional activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit. Newport warns that this skill is becoming rare at the exact moment it is becoming most valuable, while "Shallow Work" (logistical-style tasks) is taking over. 

After closing the book and clicking return on Libby, I looked at my calendar and asked a simple question: *How much deep work do I actually get done in a week?*

The answer brought me face-to-face with the **Polite Lie** of our industry: that Scrum reduces meetings and increases velocity.
The **Ugly Truth** is that Scrum has become a performance art designed to make managers feel safe while developers drown in shallow work.

I’m not sure the global corporate workplace even has a time for "Deep Work" anymore.

### The Agile Masquerade

If you are a "resource" (the corporate euphemism for a human being with a pulse and a keyboard), your day likely begins at 8:00 AM NY time. From then until 11:00 AM, you are not a developer. You are an extra in a play called *The Theatre of Agility*.

You attend the Squad Stand-up (15 minutes that takes 30). You attend the Peer Sync. The Upwards Reporting Sync. The Pre-Planning for the Planning. You move sticky notes from "To Do" to "Doing" to prove you are alive. You nod. You say "I'm unblocked."

Newport captures this performative busyness perfectly: **"In the absence of clear indicators of what it means to be productive and valuable... knowledge workers cultivate a state of visible busyness, as a proxy for productive effort."**

We have confused *activity* with *productivity*. We believe that if we are talking about work, we are working. But as Newport warns: **"If you don't produce, you won't thrive-no matter how skilled or talented you are."**

### Drowning in Shallow Waters

Then comes the afternoon shift: "Deck Building." You aren't debugging a race condition; you are aligning text boxes on a slide to explain *why* you haven't fixed the race condition yet. You are answering pings on Teams that demand immediate attention for non-immediate problems.

This constant context-switching is what Newport calls "Attention Residue". Every time you glance at a notification, your brain remains stuck on that distraction even after you turn back to your code.

**"People experiencing attention residue after switching tasks are likely to demonstrate poor performance on that next task."**

The modern workplace has become a machine for generating this residue. The urge to check Teams or Outlook becomes, in Newport's words, **"a nervous twitch that shatters uninterrupted time into shards too small to support the presence necessary for an intentional life."**

We are drowning in the shallows, mistaking the splashing for swimming.



### The Commander in the Bunker

But there is a resistance growing. It’s not happening in the conference rooms. It’s happening in the IDEs.

The closest I’ve come to Deep Work in the last year hasn't been during "No Meeting Fridays" (which are a myth). It’s been late in the evening, sitting with an Agentic Coding IDE at my kitchen table, commanding an army of AI agents, orchestrators, and tools.

This is the new reality: **The Theatre vs. The Engine.**

In the Theatre (your day job), you are a collaborator. You negotiate. You estimate points. You play the game.
In the Engine (your agentic workflow), you are a **Commander**.

When I vibe-code with an army of agents, I don't "pair program." I direct. I turn a midnight spark of creativity-a one-liner idea-into a deployed prototype in twenty minutes. I don't have to explain the syntax to the agent. I don't have to wait for the agent to finish its coffee. I just issue the order, and the code manifests.

This is the shift Newport predicted, though perhaps not in this form. **"Depth will become increasingly rare and therefore increasingly valuable."**

The value today isn't in your ability to endure a two-hour refinement session. It's in your ability to retreat to the bunker, shut out the noise, and wield these new tools to build things that the "Squad" couldn't ship in a quarter.

### Let the Small Bad Things Happen

The corporate world tells you that if you miss a meeting, the sky will fall. It won't.
They tell you that if you don't respond to that email within 10 minutes, you are "unprofessional." You aren't.

You are simply protecting the only asset you have left: your attention.

Newport advises: **"Develop the habit of letting small bad things happen. If you don't, you'll never find time for the life-changing big things."**

This is the hardest pill to swallow. We are wired to please. We are wired to be "responsive." But responsiveness is the enemy of depth. **"Clarity about what matters provides clarity about what does not."**

If you want to build the "life-changing big things"-the architecture that scales, the agentic workflow that 10x's your output-you must be willing to be a "bad" citizen of the shallow state.

Let the stand-up be awkward because you missed it. Let the Teams message sit unread for three hours. Let the "Deck" be ugly.

Retreat to the bunker. Fire up the agents. And remember what it feels like to actually *build* something again.
