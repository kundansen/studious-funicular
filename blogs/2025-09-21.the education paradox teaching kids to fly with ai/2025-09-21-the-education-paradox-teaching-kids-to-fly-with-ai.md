# The Education Paradox: Are We Teaching Kids to Fly with AI - Only to Clip Their Wings?

*September 21, 2025*

### A polished paper, a quiet room

The essay was well-organized and polished, almost too perfect to believe. Each sentence flowed smoothly, and at first, the teacher was quite impressed. However, they couldn't follow through when she asked the student to explain one of their points to the class. The words on the page seemed confident, but the voice behind them was silent. While this example was from a classroom, it's easy to imagine the same silence in tomorrow's conference room, when polished work meets the demand for live explanation.

That silence highlights an interesting paradox of AI in education. When we can bypass the tough parts of learning—the struggle, re-writing, and false starts—it's worth asking: what becomes of the strength and resilience built through doing the work ourselves?

Schools are already facing these challenges. For instance, New York City initially banned ChatGPT in classrooms but later reconsidered. Universities are encouraging students to write by hand once more. Teachers mention assignments that seem "too perfect." Knowledge, and some say even intelligence, can now be summoned instantly rather than carefully built over time. While this example was from a classroom, it's easy to imagine the same silence in tomorrow's conference room, when polished work meets the demand for live explanation.

### Haven't we worried before?

It's interesting to see how new tools have historically caused concern in classrooms, like when parents worried calculators might replace basic arithmetic, or later, when there was fear that search engines could diminish our memory. But as always, schools found their way around these worries, helping students progress from mastering long division to exploring calculus, and from simply memorizing facts to creatively putting knowledge together in new and exciting ways.

Today's AI tools are beyond the natural progression of previous evolutions. Instead of just directing you to an answer, they can actually generate one for you. Whether it's a complete essay, a poem, or even functional code, just a few words in a prompt can do it all. It's exciting to see how the line between effort and completed work is quickly becoming less distinct.

Dr. Po-Shen Loh, a math professor at Carnegie Mellon and a longtime coach of the U.S. Math Olympiad team, has been thinking about this. His solution isn't to ban AI but to design around it. His exams are open-book, open-notes, even open-deadline. The trick? The problems are brand new each time, crafted so no AI—or student—can copy. To solve them, you still have to think.

---

### The bright side, and the shadow

Watch students use AI, and you'll see two pictures at once.

On the bright side, it opens doors. A student with dyslexia finds a patient tutor. An English learner has the chance to practice conversation long after class. Tools like Khan Academy's Khanmigo or Duolingo's AI features gently guide students without simply giving away answers. For many who struggle silently, the machine offers them a helpful way forward.

But there's also a bit of a shadow to consider. Since it's so simple to use, students might rely on the tool to do the thinking they are actually meant to do themselves. Psychologists call this "cognitive outsourcing." The real concern isn't just about getting an answer—it's about missing out on the valuable growth that comes from working through the challenge.

Loh feels that honesty is key. He encourages students to openly declare when they use AI, just like citing a source. It's not about making anyone feel guilty. Instead, it's about helping students take pride in their learning journey, not just their final work.

---

### Fragile brilliance at work

This isn't only a school problem—it's a preview of what workplaces will face as today's students become tomorrow's hires. I remember a manager sharing their experience of hiring a junior developer who submitted flawless, working code. Yet, when asked to explain their work, the developer froze up. Everything looked polished on the outside, but beneath the surface, their understanding was quite limited.

Recruiters now talk about "AI-inflated résumés." They often see applications that seem impressive at first, but can feel empty underneath. It's not about dishonesty; they're more concerned about how fragile these résumés are. Brightness that might break apart when put to the test. The gap between polished output and fundamental understanding in schools foreshadows the same fragility in boardrooms, labs, and offices.

---

### Teachers in the trenches

Teachers feel the tension every day.

On one hand, assessments can sometimes feel less trustworthy. Routine problem sets or essays might not fully reveal who genuinely understands the material. This is why some universities have decided to go back to hand-written exams or require oral defenses, making sure they get a clearer picture of student understanding.

On the other hand, AI can be a helpful tool. Teachers find it useful for creating lesson plans or grading essays more quickly, which frees up more time to connect with their students. In Newark, pilots with Khanmigo have shown how kids tend to stay engaged with math problems longer, thanks to AI's encouraging guidance.

Educators like Loh find themselves right in the middle. He creates assessments that AI can't easily simplify and encourages students to be honest about when they rely on tools. This approach brings trust and openness, helping students grow and learn more effectively.

The way teachers respond now will shape not just classrooms but also the skill base of the next decade's workforce.

---

### Rethinking what counts as learning

Part of AI's appeal is how it makes you feel like you're moving forward. You ask; it answers. But learning isn't about polished answers. It's about the messy space in between—trying, failing, and trying again until something clicks.

This means schools should focus on creating the right environment for understanding, rather than only on the answers. Think of classrooms that are lively, human, and full of collaboration. Students explore ideas, share insights, and discuss different viewpoints. The AI, in this case, acts as a kind and patient guide, supporting their learning without taking over the task of creating answers. That distinction matters because the students practicing this today will soon be the professionals asked to solve problems no AI has yet seen.

---

### A playbook for resilience

So what do we do? Some principles are emerging:

- **Make work new.** Problems should be fresh enough that no AI can recycle an old answer.
- **Grade the process.** Look at drafts, notes, and reflections. Ask students to describe what AI did and what they themselves figured out.
- **Set boundaries.** Be clear about when AI is allowed, and when it's not. Clarity reduces both stress and misuse.
- **Practice without it.** Build in the moments—hand-written bursts, oral quizzes—where AI can't step in.
- **Teach the meta-skill.** Not just how to ask a model a question, but how to question its answer.

---

### Wings and muscles

AI has given students wings. The real test is whether we also help them build the muscle to use them.

Loh's approach, emphasizing tackling new challenges and being open about processes, provides a meaningful path forward. UNESCO and many others share this view: AI should serve to enhance human learning rather than take it over.

The paradox isn't about whether AI will change education; it already has. The real question is whether we can nurture the ongoing effort—the stumbling, the stretching, the late-night wrestling—because that's where real strength develops. Wings without muscle are just decoration, and decoration doesn't fly in classrooms—or companies that depend on resilient thinkers.
