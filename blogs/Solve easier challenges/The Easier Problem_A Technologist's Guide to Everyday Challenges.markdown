# The Easier Problem: A Technologist's Guide to Everyday Challenges

Thirty years ago, I hit a wall during a math exam that still lingers in my memory. The probability and statistics section was a maze of confusion, and I was lost in it. In a last-ditch effort, I flipped the script: I invented my own questions and solved them, leaving a sheepish note for the professor. It was a gamble, but it paid off—I passed. More than that, it revealed a truth I’ve carried into my career: when a problem feels insurmountable, there’s always a simpler starting point if you look for it.

This approach isn’t just a quirky anecdote. It’s rooted in George Polya’s timeless problem-solving framework from *How to Solve It* and enriched by Daniel Kahneman’s insights on thinking from *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Together, they offer a roadmap for technologists facing the daily grind of complex challenges—without losing your sanity or your sense of humor.

## What Polya Knew: Start Small, Win Big

George Polya had a knack for cutting through chaos. His advice? “If you can’t solve a problem, then there is an easier problem you can solve—find it.” It’s deceptively simple, but it works. Back in that exam, I didn’t know Polya by name, but I was channeling his wisdom. By tackling questions I could handle, I built a bridge to understanding the mess in front of me.

For technologists, this translates directly to the workplace. Debugging a sprawling codebase? Don’t stare at the whole beast—zero in on one function and test it. Progress isn’t about heroics; it’s about momentum. And sometimes, that first small win is all you need to keep going.

## Fast vs. Slow: Kahneman’s Two-Speed Mind

Daniel Kahneman’s work adds depth to this strategy. He describes two thinking modes: System 1, the fast, gut-driven responder, and System 2, the slower, analytical problem-solver. During my exam meltdown, System 1 was screaming, *You’re doomed!* But System 2 stepped in, calmly suggesting, *Let’s work with what we’ve got.*

In tech, we live this tension daily. Deadlines push us toward quick fixes (System 1), but sticky problems—like a mysterious bug or a tricky API integration—demand the deliberate focus of System 2. The trick is knowing when to shift gears. Rushing might feel productive, but slowing down often gets you there faster. Ever tried fixing a glitch in a caffeine-fueled frenzy, only to realize you missed the obvious? Exactly.

## How Does This Help Me Code Better?

Let’s get practical—how do these ideas apply to your next project? When a task looms large, Polya’s “easier problem” tactic can be your lifeline. Say you’re building a new feature and the requirements feel like a Gordian knot. Start by prototyping a single piece—like a button that triggers one action. It’s not the whole solution, but it’s a foothold.

Kahneman’s lens helps too. Before diving in, pause. Sketch out the logic or talk it through with a teammate. This isn’t procrastination—it’s priming your brain to think clearly. And when you’re stuck, don’t bang your head against the monitor. Step back, simplify, and ask: What’s the smallest thing I can solve right now? Nine times out of ten, that’s enough to crack the bigger puzzle.

## Collaboration: Dividing the Complexity

Teams can amplify this approach. A massive project can overwhelm even the sharpest minds, but break it into bite-sized problems, and suddenly it’s manageable. Assign each piece to someone’s strengths—one person tackles the database, another the UI—and watch the chaos turn into clarity.

I once worked on a deadline-crushed app rollout where nothing was working. Instead of flailing as a group, we split up: I debugged the backend while a colleague mocked up the frontend. We weren’t geniuses; we just found our easier problems. By the end, we had a working prototype—and a story for the next team happy hour.

## The Takeaway: You’ve Got This

Here’s the bottom line: complex challenges don’t require superhuman brilliance. They need a willingness to start small and think smart. Polya gives you the strategy; Kahneman gives you the mindset. Together, they’re a one-two punch for any technologist’s toolkit.

So, next time you’re staring down a problem that feels like it’s mocking you, don’t despair. Find the easier problem, solve it, and build from there. You might not rewrite the history of computing, but you’ll get the job done—and maybe even have a laugh along the way.
