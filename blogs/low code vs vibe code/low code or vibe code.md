## The Blog Post

There’s a familiar meme that keeps appearing online (originating from Spiderman 2002). Two characters face each other across what looks like a deep divide. Different tribes. Different worldviews. Then, somewhere in the exchange, one of them pauses and says, almost reluctantly, “We are not so different.”

That line has been echoing in my head as I’ve watched two movements in software development mature in parallel. On one side, low-code and no-code platforms allow non technical folks to build professional looking applications by clicking and dragging. On the other, a new class of AI-enabled coding engines allow erstwhile developers to build entire applications by typing (or even speaking) to AI orchestration workflows and develop fully fleshed applications without actually writing a line of code.

At first glance, these worlds look opposed. Look a little longer, and it becomes clear they’re two sides of the same coin - neither is actually writing a line of code, and both are producing crisp professional-grade software systems.

---

### The real bottleneck was never code

For years, we’ve framed software productivity around effort. Lines written. Tickets closed. Timelines colored and re-finished in a dozen ways. Velocity charts polished for quarterly reviews. I feel effort was never the fundamental constraint - latency was.

The time between an idea forming and something tangible existing is where momentum leaks away. Meetings intervene. Calendars fill. Context thins. Enthusiasm wanes via waiting. Ideas fade from memory. By the time a prototype appears, past Jiras and prioritizations and planning pokers and holidays and vacations, the original spark often cools into a polite indifference.

Early AI-assisted coding felt quietly radical. The code was far from perfect, but the delay started to collapse. 

It began modestly. An inline suggestion. A bracket you didn’t have to type. A comment—“sort this list using quicksort”—that turned into executable logic. Small moments, but psychologically meaningful ones. It felt like we were living the future. Now we know, it was the beginning of the end of hand-written code.

Then, entire functions started getting generated via a few lines of English. Then classes. Then files. Waiting a few seconds for a coherent block of reasoning felt normal. Remembering how Bubble Sort works became less and less relevant - perhaps only for the recent college grad looking for the first job interview. What really stood out was the quality—code that was often cleaner, better commented, and more consistent than what many early-career developers might produce under time pressure. It really highlights the dedication and skill involved.

Impressive, yes, but still just the opening act.

### When tools stopped behaving like tools

The paradigm shift arrived with agentic coding systems. These didn’t just extend autocomplete - they changed the shape of work.

Suddenly, systems could reason across hundreds of files in a codebase, understand architectural intent, write tests, run them, fail, fix themselves, integrate with CI pipelines, and repeat. Maker–checker loops emerged organically. Progress replaced output as the unit of value. Human developers started drinking more coffee as they needed to do something when their AI coding assistants were busy building applications.

In the hands of an experienced technologist—someone fluent in system design, integration seams, and the subtle tells of bad abstractions (see my blog from last week on thin slicing)—these systems compress time in ways that are hard to appreciate from the outside. Work that would take teams of junior developers months can emerge in hours, not because corners were cut, but because coordination overhead disappeared. Thoughts-to-code is the new farm-to-table.

Then, AI attention spans expanded once more, going from seconds to minutes, then hours, and even whole days. Codex can keep working for over 7 hours straight, thanks to a team of specialized agents and solid orchestration. There are no stand-ups, no handoffs, and no loss of momentum—making the process smoother and more efficient.

At that point, you’re no longer writing code. You’re shaping intent. You’re steering architecture. You’re validating direction. Thoughts turn into systems. Words turn into running software. "Fail fast" and "shift left" have never carried so much weight and impact.

That’s what I like to call vibe-coding. It's not about being sloppy or just waving hands around. Instead, it’s about having a clear high-level intent, paired with quick, tangible feedback. It's like improvising within some helpful guardrails. Judgment is enhanced and sharpened, not ignored or bypassed.

### What low-code genuinely got right

As this evolution continues, there's also another movement that truly deserves our admiration. Low-code and no-code platforms haven't just promised faster development; they've opened the door for more people, particularly from non-coding backgrounds, to get involved in building software.

For many types of challenges—like approval workflows, internal dashboards, or simple automations—this was the perfect way to approach things. Just click instead of typing, drag instead of coding up complex interfaces. It helps you achieve professional results without having to think like a software engineer. Take Microsoft Power Platform, for example; it makes it quite hard to create a UI that doesn’t look good.

The only thing to keep in mind is that the limitation isn’t failure, but rather the scope. Understanding this can open up new possibilities and help us focus on what truly matters.

### The phone camera problem

A modern phone camera is astonishing. In good light, it produces results that would have required serious equipment not long ago. Tap, shoot, share. Minimal friction.

But step outside those conditions and the illusion fades. Try a dark room with a single candle. Try long exposures. Try star trails. The phone does its best, but it’s operating beyond what it was designed for.

That’s when the dedicated camera—the one that looks heavier, slower, and more complex—suddenly becomes more efficient. Not because it’s easier, but because it’s at home in that harsh lighting environment.

Low-code platforms, similarly, work really well on simple, straightforward projects. However, when you venture into more complex territory—like custom layouts, unusual flows, or unique logic—some of their limitations become noticeable. You might see stylesheets appear, templates breaking, or integration points emerging that weren’t originally intended. It’s all part of the journey as things get a bit more intricate.

What was “no-code” quietly becomes some-code, or even lot of code, except now you’re navigating someone else’s assumptions and design guardrails.

### Efficiency is contextual

There’s a well-known automotive experiment by Top Gear in the mid-2000's: take a Prius hybrid and a BMW M3 performance sports sedan to a racetrack. Drive them hard. Counterintuitively, the M3 proves more efficient on the track than the Prius. It's because the M3 was in its comfort zone, cruising around the S curves, while the Prius was redlining to keep up.

Efficiency only makes sense in context.

Think of low-code and no-code frameworks as comfortable commuter cars, easy to use and perfect for everyday tasks. AI-driven coding systems are like powerful performance cars, built for high efficiency and speed. Both have their unique strengths, and each one is just right for different kinds of journeys.

The mistake is assuming one should replace the other everywhere.

### Why speed changes psychology

This is where ideas from Malcolm Gladwell's The Tipping Point become unexpectedly relevant, on the chapter about Sesame Street experiments on stickiness and distraction. 

Ideas are fragile early on. They don’t fail because they’re flawed. They fail because they’re slow to turn into implementations. Quoting Jurassic Park - Life, uh, finds a way (to slow you down). Latency creates space for distraction, doubt, fading memories, and dilution.

When you’re able to go from a rough idea to a working prototype in just an evening, it’s a pretty special moment. That idea still feels fresh, and skepticism hasn’t taken hold yet. You’re still emotionally connected to it. That’s the quiet strength of vibe-coding with AI agents. You share your intent, and the system brings it to life. You respond, and it adapts — creating a smooth, tight feedback loop where distractions have little chance to interfere.

Traditional development cycles—and even many low-code initiatives—operate on a different rhythm. They require coordination, calendars, and alignment across time zones. By the time a prototype appears, the energy that birthed it has often dissipated.

The artifact survives. The idea, in its original form, often doesn’t.

### Where this shows up in real life

For me, this doesn’t happen during Zoom calls. It usually happens at the kitchen counter — a cozy spot where a glass of wine sits between dinner and bedtime, with a laptop open and an AI agent waiting for the next instruction on VSCode. It’s not about working more; it’s about savoring those moments when ideas come to life. If it's really serious coding, I switch to an Old Fashioned so the spherical ice ball gets a chance to melt while I watch my virtual team of coders bang out the implementation. Low-code platforms are helpful, reliable, and efficient, but they don't feel as inspiring. Watching an AI transform your words into a working solution, however, captures that magical sense of play. This feeling isn’t just a luxury — it’s a powerful spark that boosts your creativity and motivation.

### Not a verdict. A reframing.

This is less of a winner-takes-all story. Low-code and no-code platforms will continue to grow, especially in areas where having clear structure and predictability matters most. At the same time, AI-powered, agentic coding will give individuals and small teams the ability to experiment and innovate more easily and creatively, unlocking exciting new opportunities.

The true skill lies in selecting the right environments, not just picking sides. It’s about creating spaces where you can thrive and feel comfortable.

**You do low-code. I pro-code in vibe-code. We are not so different.**

We’re just driving different machines, on different roads, trying to get ideas safely across the fragile space between imagination and reality.
